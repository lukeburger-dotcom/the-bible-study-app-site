## Direct Answer

Isaiah 7:14 uses the Hebrew word עַלְמָה (almah), meaning "young woman of marriageable age" — a term that does not itself specify virginity, though it can describe a virgin, since in ancient Israelite culture an unmarried young woman was presumptively a virgin. The Septuagint (LXX), translated some centuries before Christ, rendered almah with παρθένος (parthenos), the standard Greek word for "virgin." Matthew 1:23 quotes the LXX's parthenos directly and applies the prophecy to Mary's virginal conception of Jesus. The interpretive question turns on whether Isaiah's original audience would have understood almah as necessarily implying virginity, and how the sign functions in its 8th-century-BC setting before it reaches its fuller Matthean fulfillment.

## Original-Language Notes

I chose two words for this study — almah and parthenos — because the entire historical debate lives in the semantic gap (or lack thereof) between them, and a third, betulah, because it's the Hebrew word interpreters say Isaiah could have used instead but didn't.

| Range of meaning | BDB and HALOT both give the core sense as "young woman," specifically one who has reached puberty and is of marriageable age; the term does not carry an explicit grammatical marker of virginity the way betulah can in certain contexts. HALOT notes the word occurs rarely in the Hebrew Bible (seven times), including Genesis 24:43 (Rebekah) and Song of Solomon 1:3, 6:8. |
| Semantic domain | Age/marital status terminology; social classification of unmarried women |
| Etymology | Almah shares a root with the word for youthful vigor/puberty, but etymology alone cannot settle its precise meaning in this text — word history is not the same as word usage, and a word's sense is governed by how it is actually used in context, not by its root. |

Almah's ambiguity is the crux of the whole debate: BDB and HALOT agree it denotes a young, marriageable woman without making virginity the explicit lexical content of the word — yet in Israelite social reality, an unmarried young woman of this description would ordinarily be assumed a virgin. This is why interpreters disagree sharply on whether Isaiah is asserting virginity as such or simply identifying a specific young woman (perhaps in Ahaz's court) whose child's birth and naming would function as a timing-sign.

Some interpreters (e.g., Edward J. Young, *The Book of Isaiah*, New International Commentary on the Old Testament, 1965) argue that if Isaiah had wanted to unambiguously assert virginity, betulah was the more natural word available, and that his choice of almah instead is significant. Others (e.g., John Oswalt, *The Book of Isaiah 1–39*, New International Commentary on the Old Testament, 1986) note that betulah itself is not always unambiguous either (cf. Joel 1:8, where a betulah mourns "the husband of her youth"), so the argument from word choice alone cannot fully settle the question.

| Sense here | A woman who has not had sexual intercourse; a virgin |
| Range of meaning | BDAG gives the primary sense as "one who has never engaged in sexual intercourse," used of both women and (rarely) men in Greek literature; Louw-Nida places it in the domain of kinship/marital status terms specifically denoting virginity. |

Parthenos is a much less ambiguous word than almah — the standard Koine Greek lexicons treat it as specifically denoting virginity, not merely youth or marital status. This is why the LXX translators' choice of parthenos for almah in Isaiah 7:14 is theologically significant: whatever Isaiah's Hebrew audience understood, the Greek-speaking Jewish translators of the LXX (produced roughly in the last few centuries BC) rendered the term in a way that made virginity explicit, and Matthew inherits and builds directly on that Greek rendering rather than translating the Hebrew afresh.

## Context & Background

**Historical setting.** Isaiah 7 is set during the Syro-Ephraimite crisis (c. 734–732 BC), when King Ahaz of Judah faced an alliance of Syria (Aram) and the northern kingdom of Israel (Ephraim) seeking to depose him. According to J. Alec Motyer, *The Prophecy of Isaiah* (1993), Isaiah confronts Ahaz with an offer of a divine sign to confirm that the threatening coalition would fail, and Ahaz refuses under a pretense of piety (Isaiah 7:12), prompting Isaiah's rebuke and the giving of the sign anyway.

**The sign's original function.** In its immediate 8th-century context, the sign concerns timing: before a child (the almah's son) reaches the age of moral discernment, the two threatening kings' territories would be desolated (Isaiah 7:15–16). Commentators disagree on the child's identity — proposals include a son of Isaiah, a son of Ahaz (later Hezekiah), or a more generically described child in the near future (see Oswalt, *The Book of Isaiah 1–39*, 1986, for a survey of options).

**The Septuagint's role.** The Septuagint (LXX) is a Greek translation of the Hebrew Scriptures produced by Jewish translators, with the Pentateuch traditionally dated to the 3rd century BC and other books, including Isaiah, following in subsequent centuries BC. Its rendering of almah as parthenos in Isaiah 7:14 reflects a translational choice made by Jewish scholars centuries before Christ and well before any Christian use of the text — a point often raised by commentators (e.g., Motyer, 1993) against the charge that parthenos was a distinctively Christian imposition on the passage.

**Matthew's use.** Matthew, writing his Gospel to a Jewish-Christian audience, employs a recurring "fulfillment" formula ("that it might be fulfilled which was spoken...") throughout his infancy narrative (cf. Matthew 1:22, 2:15, 2:17, 2:23). According to Craig Blomberg, *Matthew* (New American Commentary, 1992), Matthew's use of Isaiah 7:14 reflects a "fuller sense" (sensus plenior) reading in which the original oracle's near-term sign to Ahaz finds its ultimate, escalated fulfillment in the virginal conception of Jesus — a pattern of prophecy and heightened fulfillment common in Second Temple Jewish and early Christian exegesis.

**Comparative and material context.** There is no significant archaeological or material-culture evidence bearing directly on the almah/parthenos question itself; the debate is primarily lexical, textual, and theological rather than archaeological.

## Interpretive Options

1. **Virgin birth as the primary sense in Isaiah, fulfilled typologically/directly in Christ.** Representative: Edward J. Young (*The Book of Isaiah*, NICOT, 1965); Motyer (1993). This view holds that almah in context already implies virginity (given the extraordinary nature of a "sign"), and that Matthew's use is a direct, intended fulfillment, not merely an analogical application.

2. **"Young woman" as the primary 8th-century referent, with Matthew applying a fuller/typological sense.** Representative: John Oswalt (*The Book of Isaiah 1–39*, NICOT, 1986); many historical-critical commentators. This view holds that Isaiah's almah likely referred to a specific young woman known to Ahaz's court (not necessarily a virgin in the miraculous sense), whose child's birth served as a near-term timing sign; Matthew's citation operates on a "pattern fulfillment" or sensus plenior basis rather than claiming Isaiah predicted a literal virgin birth 700 years in advance.

3. **A view emphasizing the LXX's mediating role.** Some scholars (surveyed in Oswalt, 1986) note that regardless of the original Hebrew nuance, Matthew is working from the Greek text already available to him, in which parthenos had settled the question lexically — so Matthew's argument does not require resolving the Hebrew ambiguity itself, only the Greek wording he inherited.

These views are not always mutually exclusive; many evangelical commentators (e.g., Motyer, Blomberg) hold both that Isaiah's own language plausibly pointed toward virginity and that the ultimate fulfillment is in Christ, while critical commentators more often separate the "original sense" sharply from Matthew's Christian application.
