## Direct Answer
Matthew 6:33 calls disciples to make God's kingdom and righteousness their controlling priority, trusting Him to supply material needs as a consequence — not as the goal itself. It caps Jesus' teaching against anxiety about food, drink, and clothing (Matthew 6:25–34), reorienting worry into trust.

## Key Passage(s)

> But seek first His kingdom and His righteousness, and all these things will be added to you. (Matthew 6:33, BSB)

> But seek ye first the kingdom of God, and his righteousness; and all these things shall be added unto you. (Matthew 6:33, KJV)

Surrounding context:

## Original-Language Notes
Two words carry the weight here: ζητεῖτε ("seek"), because its imperative form and word-order placement make priority the whole point, and δικαιοσύνην ("righteousness"), because it's a loaded term Matthew uses elsewhere for both God's covenant faithfulness and the conduct He requires of disciples.

### ζητεῖτε (zēteite)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Matthew 6:33 — "But seek" |
| Lemma | ζητέω |
| Parsing | present active imperative, 2nd person plural |
| Lexicon refs | BDAG 1; Louw-Nida 27.1 |
| Sense here | to earnestly pursue or make the object of one's aim |

The present tense imperative signals continuous, ongoing pursuit — not a one-time decision. Its placement first in the Greek clause (πρῶτον, "first," modifies the whole action) underscores priority over process: this is the thing disciples keep pursuing above all competing pursuits, including the food/clothing anxieties just named.

### δικαιοσύνην (dikaiosynēn)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Matthew 6:33 — "His righteousness" |
| Lemma | δικαιοσύνη |
| Parsing | noun, accusative singular feminine |
| Lexicon refs | BDAG 3; TDNT |
| Sense here | right conduct/character conforming to God's will |

Matthew uses this term repeatedly in the Sermon on the Mount (5:6, 5:10, 5:20, 6:1) for the righteousness of kingdom citizens — a life increasingly aligned with God's character and commands, not merely a legal status. D.A. Carson, Matthew (Expositor's Bible Commentary, 1984), reads it here as the practical righteousness the Sermon has been describing throughout chapters 5–6.

Want the word behind "kingdom" (βασιλεία) or "added" (προστεθήσεται)? Just ask.

## Context & Background
Matthew 6:25–34 sits within the Sermon on the Mount, addressed to disciples (5:1–2) rather than crowds generally. Craig Blomberg, Matthew (New American Commentary, 1992), notes the passage responds to genuine anxiety over subsistence-level needs common in first-century Galilean peasant life, where daily wages and daily bread were the norm for much of the population — not abstract worry but real economic precarity.

The "birds" and "lilies" illustrations (6:26, 28) draw on wisdom-literature style nature analogies, a common teaching device, per R.T. France, The Gospel of Matthew (New International Commentary on the New Testament, 2007). The command not to serve "God and mammon" (6:24) immediately precedes this section, framing verse 33 as the resolution to a divided-loyalty problem: anxiety over provision functionally competes with singular devotion to God.

## Interpretive Options
- **Priority, not exclusion**: Jesus does not forbid working or planning for material needs, but subordinates them under kingdom-seeking as the organizing priority. This is a common reading among evangelical commentators including Blomberg and Carson (cited above).
- **Eschatological kingdom emphasis**: Some readings (e.g., George Eldon Ladd, A Theology of the New Testament, 1974) stress that "kingdom" here carries Matthew's characteristic sense of God's dynamic reign breaking in through Jesus, making the verse as much about allegiance to Christ's present reign as about future provision.
- **"Righteousness" as covenant faithfulness vs. personal ethics**: Some scholars debate whether δικαιοσύνη leans toward God's saving righteousness/vindication or human ethical conduct; Matthew's usage elsewhere (5:20) favors the latter within this Gospel's context, per France.
