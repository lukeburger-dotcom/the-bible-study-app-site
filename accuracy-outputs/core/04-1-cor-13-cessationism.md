## Direct Answer

1 Corinthians 13:8–13 says love never fails, while prophecies, tongues, and knowledge will "pass away" or "cease" when "the perfect" (or "that which is perfect") comes. Whether this supports cessationism — the view that certain spiritual gifts (especially prophecy and tongues) ceased after the apostolic age or with the completion of the New Testament canon — is genuinely contested among careful interpreters. The text itself does not name the canon, the apostolic age, or a specific historical moment; it identifies "the perfect" only by contrast with "the partial" and by the analogy of seeing "in a mirror dimly" versus seeing "face to face." Cessationists (e.g., Richard Gaffin) and continuationists (e.g., D.A. Carson, Wayne Grudem) both affirm the text but disagree sharply on what "the perfect" refers to and therefore on what the passage implies for today.

## Original-Language Notes

Two terms carry the weight of this debate: **τὸ τέλειον** ("the perfect/complete"), because the whole cessationist-continuationist argument hinges on identifying its referent, and **καταργηθήσεται/καταργηθήσονται** ("will pass away/be done away"), because the verb's force shapes whether Paul describes gradual obsolescence or abrupt termination.

### τέλειον (teleion)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | 1 Corinthians 13:10 — "the perfect" |
| Lemma | τέλειος |
| Parsing | adjective, neuter singular, nominative (substantival, functioning as a noun) |
| Lexicon refs | BDAG; Louw-Nida 65.19; 88.99 |
| Sense here | "complete, whole, mature, fully developed" — contrasted with τὸ ἐκ μέρους, "the partial/incomplete" |
| Range of meaning | Can mean moral/ethical maturity or perfection (as in Matthew 5:48), completeness of a process or object, or full growth/maturity (as of an adult versus a child, cf. 1 Corinthians 14:20). Does not inherently mean "the canon of Scripture" or "the return of Christ" — those are inferences drawn from context, not from the word itself. |
| Semantic domain | Completeness/wholeness; also used in the domain of maturity (contrasted with childhood, νήπιος) |
| Etymology | Related to τέλος ("end, goal, purpose"), but the sense here should not be read as "the ending thing" merely from the root — usage in context, not derivation, governs meaning (etymological fallacy caution). |

This word is the crux of the whole debate. Paul never defines it explicitly in this passage; interpreters supply the referent (Scripture's completion, the eschaton, the mature church) based on theological framework rather than lexical necessity.

### καταργηθήσεται (katargēthēsetai)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | 1 Corinthians 13:8 — "they will pass away" (of prophecies) / "will cease" (of tongues) |
| Lemma | καταργέω |
| Parsing | future passive indicative, 3rd person singular (of προφητεῖαι) |
| Lexicon refs | BDAG; Louw-Nida 13.98; 68.41 |
| Sense here | "to be rendered inoperative, abolished, brought to an end" |
| Range of meaning | Paul uses this verb elsewhere for the Law's binding power ending (Romans 7:2, 6), for the "old covenant" glory fading (2 Corinthians 3:7, 11, 13-14), and for death being "destroyed" (1 Corinthians 15:26). It denotes decisive nullification rather than gradual fading, though context determines the manner. |
| Semantic domain | Cessation, nullification, rendering ineffective |
| Etymology | From κατά (intensive) + ἀργέω ("be idle/inactive," itself from ἀργός, "not working"). The compound intensifies the base sense but should not be pressed into "utterly and completely destroyed" beyond what usage supports. |

Notably, Paul uses a *different* verb, παύσονται ("will cease," middle voice), for tongues specifically in verse 8, which some interpreters (e.g., Robert Thomas) argue is significant — a nuance the English "cease" alone doesn't flag against "pass away." Both verbs, however, describe the *same event* — the coming of τὸ τέλειον — so most interpreters across positions do not treat the verb shift as independently decisive.

Want the word behind "in part" (ἐκ μέρους) or "face to face" (πρόσωπον πρὸς πρόσωπον)? Just ask.

## Context & Background

**Literary setting.** 1 Corinthians 13 sits between chapters 12 and 14, both of which address spiritual gifts (χαρίσματα) in the Corinthian assembly — chapter 12 lists and ranks gifts, chapter 14 regulates tongues and prophecy in worship. Chapter 13 functions as Paul's argument that love is the necessary framework for exercising any gift; this is widely noted in the commentary tradition (e.g., Gordon Fee, *The First Epistle to the Corinthians*, New International Commentary on the New Testament, 1987; Anthony Thiselton, *The First Epistle to the Corinthians*, New International Greek Testament Commentary, 2000).

**Historical situation.** Corinth was a Roman colony (re-founded 44 B.C.) and major commercial hub in Achaia, known for wealth, status-competition, and religious pluralism, including mystery-cult ecstatic practices — a backdrop scholars like Fee and Thiselton use to explain the Corinthians' fascination with tongues as a status marker. Paul is writing to correct disorder and status-seeking in the use of gifts, not primarily to give a timeline for their cessation (Fee, 1987).

**Comparative background.** Some scholars note parallels between Corinthian ecstatic speech and Greco-Roman oracular or mystery-religion phenomena (discussed cautiously by Thiselton, 2000, who warns against over-drawing the parallel), though Paul's theological framework (gifts given by the Spirit for the building up of the church, 1 Corinthians 12:7) is distinctly rooted in Jewish-Christian pneumatology rather than pagan analogues.

**The "child to adult" and "mirror" images.** Paul's illustrations (vv. 11-12) draw on common household experience (maturation from childhood) and on the ancient use of polished metal mirrors, which gave an imperfect, distorted reflection compared to direct sight — Corinth was itself known in antiquity for bronze production, making the mirror image locally resonant (noted by Fee, 1987, and Thiselton, 2000, drawing on classical sources describing the imperfection of ancient mirrors compared to modern glass).

## Interpretive Options

**1. Cessationist reading: "the perfect" = the completed New Testament canon.**
Associated with covenant theology and some Reformed traditions historically (though not exclusively). On this view, "the partial" gifts (prophecy, tongues, word of knowledge) were provisional revelatory gifts given before Scripture was complete; once the canon closed, these sign-gifts ceased because their revelatory function was fulfilled. Richard Gaffin (*Perspectives on Pentecost*, 1979) argues a version of this position, though he grounds cessation more in redemptive-historical arguments about the apostolic office than in this text alone, and is cautious about resting the case solely on 1 Corinthians 13. B.B. Warfield's classic cessationism (*Counterfeit Miracles*, 1918) likewise argues gifts were tied to apostolic authentication rather than resting the case on "the perfect" in this passage.

**2. Cessationist-adjacent reading: "the perfect" = a completed, mature state achieved historically (varies).**
A minority reading (associated in part with some dispensational cessationists) has argued "the perfect" refers to a mature state of the church or the completion of revelation more broadly construed — functionally similar to view 1 but without tying it strictly to a "closed canon" concept as such.

**3. Continuationist / eschatological reading: "the perfect" = the return of Christ / the consummated kingdom.**
This is the majority position among modern commentators across theological traditions — argued at length by Gordon Fee (1987), Anthony Thiselton (2000), D.A. Carson (*Showing the Spirit*, 1987), and Wayne Grudem (*Systematic Theology*, 1994; also his essay work on spiritual gifts). On this view, the contrast in verse 12 ("now... then," "in part... face to face," "know in part... know fully, even as I am fully known") describes the eschatological transition from this present age to the age to come at Christ's return — not a transition within church history to a completed canon. Carson and Grudem argue that "face to face" and "know fully, even as I am fully known" describe a relational immediacy with God that the possession of a complete Bible does not itself provide; the language points to the eschaton, not to a library.

**4. Church-historical / patristic-adjacent view: partial fulfillment, not a single named event.**
Some interpreters (e.g., certain strands within Thiselton's survey, 2000) note that Paul's own point may be less concerned with pinpointing *which* future moment than with establishing that *love*, unlike the gifts, is permanent — making the identification of "the perfect" secondary to Paul's rhetorical purpose. On this reading, over-specifying "the perfect" (whether as canon or parousia) risks missing the passage's actual argument: gifts are temporary and partial by nature; love is not.

**Assessment of the cessationist argument specifically.** Both Fee (1987) and Carson (1987) — writing from different theological locations but converging here — argue that reading "the perfect" as the closed canon imports a category (a completed set of documents) that is foreign to the passage's own vocabulary and imagery ("face to face," "know fully as I am known"). Gaffin's cessationism, by contrast, is typically built on broader arguments about the uniqueness of the apostolic foundation (Ephesians 2:20) rather than resting primary weight on 1 Corinthians 13:10 itself. This is worth noting: even many cessationist theologians do not consider this specific text their strongest exegetical foundation.
