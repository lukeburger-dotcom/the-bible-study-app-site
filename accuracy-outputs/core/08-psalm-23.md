## Direct Answer

Psalm 23 is a psalm of trust, portraying the LORD as a shepherd who provides, guides, and protects, and then as a gracious host who welcomes the psalmist into his house. It is attributed to David and is among the most widely loved passages in the Psalter, moving from pastoral imagery (shepherd and sheep) to a banquet scene (host and guest) while sustaining one theme: confident trust in God's care through both provision and danger.

## Original-Language Notes

Two words carry the weight of this psalm: **רָעָה** (ra'ah), the verb behind "shepherd," which shapes the whole first half, and **חֶסֶד** (chesed), rendered "lovingkindness" or "mercy," which anchors the psalm's closing confidence.

### רֹעִי (ro'i)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Psalm 23:1 — "The LORD is my shepherd" |
| Lemma | רָעָה (ra'ah) |
| Parsing | Qal participle, construct with 1st-person suffix |
| Lexicon refs | BDB; HALOT |
| Sense here | "one who shepherds/tends" — active, ongoing care, not just a title |

The participle form means David isn't merely naming God's role but describing continuous action — God is actively shepherding, right now. HALOT and BDB both note the term's regular use across the ancient Near East for kings and deities as caretakers of their people, which gives the metaphor political and covenantal weight beyond pastoral sentiment.

### חֶסֶד (chesed)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Psalm 23:6 — "lovingkindness" (BSB) / "mercy" (KJV) |
| Lemma | חֶסֶד (chesed) |
| Parsing | Noun, masculine singular |
| Lexicon refs | BDB; HALOT |
| Sense here | covenant loyalty, steadfast love bound up with relationship |

This is why BSB and KJV diverge sharply — "lovingkindness" versus "mercy" — because chesed resists a single English equivalent. BDB and HALOT describe it as loyal love rooted in covenant relationship, not mere pity or kindness in the abstract; it's the term used of God's committed faithfulness to Israel throughout the Old Testament.

## Context & Background

Psalm 23 is attributed to David in its superscription, and many interpreters connect its shepherd imagery to David's own early life tending sheep (1 Samuel 16:11; 17:34–35), as noted by Derek Kidner, *Psalms 1–72* (Tyndale Old Testament Commentaries, 1973). Shepherding was a common ancient Near Eastern metaphor for kingship and divine care, and the psalm's shift in verse 5 to a host preparing a table draws on customs of hospitality in which a host's provision — food, oil, an overflowing cup — signaled honor and protection extended to a guest, even one facing hostile surroundings ("in the presence of my enemies"). This is a general view, not a specific citation for every detail.

## Interpretive Options

- **Unified pastoral-royal image**: Some commentators, such as Kidner, read the shepherd and host images as complementary pictures of the same covenant King who both leads his flock and welcomes them at his table.
- **Structural division**: Others, including Peter Craigie, *Psalms 1–50* (Word Biblical Commentary, 1983), note the shift from third-person description ("He leads me") to second-person address ("You are with me") in verse 4, suggesting the psalm moves from confession about God to direct address to God as danger intensifies.
- **Liturgical/temple setting**: Some scholars suggest verse 6's "house of the LORD" points to temple worship and pilgrimage, framing the whole psalm as processional confidence in approaching God's presence. This is a general view, not a specific citation.
