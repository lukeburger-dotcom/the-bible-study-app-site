## Direct Answer

Daniel 7 presents a vision of four beasts (representing successive kingdoms) followed by a heavenly courtroom scene in which "one like a son of man" comes with the clouds of heaven to the Ancient of Days and receives an everlasting kingdom. This figure and title become the single most important self-designation Jesus uses for himself in the Gospels — used more than any other title, and almost exclusively by Jesus himself rather than by others about him. The dating of Daniel (6th century BC vs. a 2nd century BC composition) is a genuinely contested historical-critical question, argued on different grounds by different scholars, and I'll lay out that debate honestly below. Whatever one concludes about dating, the text's influence on New Testament Christology is direct and traceable: Jesus repeatedly draws on Daniel 7's imagery — clouds, glory, kingdom, judgment — to describe his own identity and mission, most explicitly at his trial before the Sanhedrin.

## Original-Language Notes

I've chosen **בַּר אֱנָשׁ** (Daniel's Aramaic phrase) and **ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ ἀνθρώπου** (the Gospels' fixed Greek rendering) because the shift from an indefinite comparative phrase in Aramaic to a definite title in Greek is the single biggest interpretive hinge in this whole trajectory — everything about whether Jesus is "claiming Daniel 7" turns on it.

### כְּבַר אֱנָשׁ (kevar 'enash)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Daniel 7:13 — "One like the Son of Man" |
| Lemma | בַּר אֱנָשׁ (bar 'enash) |
| Parsing | Aramaic construct noun phrase, "son of man/mankind," with prefixed comparative particle כְּ ("like/as") |
| Lexicon refs | BDB (under אֱנָשׁ, Aramaic); HALOT (Aramaic section) |
| Sense here | "one like a human being" — a figure with human appearance, distinguished from the beast-like figures earlier in the vision |
| Range of meaning | In Aramaic idiom "son of man" ordinarily just means "a human being" (cf. its parallel use in Hebrew, e.g. Ezekiel's frequent address as "son of man" = mortal); it is not, in Daniel's own language, a fixed title |
| Semantic domain | Human identity / class-membership terminology |
| Etymology | "Son of" + "man" is a common Semitic idiom for class-membership (a "son of man" = a member of humanity), not a compound implying literal parentage; etymology alone cannot tell us Daniel intended a technical title — that judgment rests on literary and canonical context, not word-parts. |

In Daniel's own Aramaic, the phrase is deliberately indefinite and comparative — "one *like* a son of man," set against beasts that are *like* a lion, a bear, a leopard. The figure's humanity is what marks him off from the empires below. Whether this figure is an individual (angelic, messianic) or a symbol for "the saints of the Most High" collectively is itself debated within Daniel 7 (see Interpretive Options).

### ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ ἀνθρώπου (ho huios tou anthrōpou)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Matthew 26:64 — "coming on the clouds of heaven," said of "the Son of Man" |
| Lemma | υἱός + ἄνθρωπος |
| Parsing | Nominative, with definite article ὁ — a fixed, articular title |
| Lexicon refs | BDAG (υἱός, sense on "Son of Man" as self-designation of Jesus); Louw-Nida 9.1 |
| Sense here | A self-designation Jesus uses almost exclusively of himself, evoking Daniel 7's exalted, cloud-riding, judgment-receiving figure |
| Range of meaning | In the Gospels the phrase functions as a fixed title with three broad usage-clusters scholars identify: (1) Jesus' earthly authority/ministry, (2) his suffering, (3) his future coming in glory/judgment — this third cluster draws most directly on Daniel 7 |
| Semantic domain | Christological title; eschatological judge-figure |
| Etymology | The Greek is a wooden, literal translation of the Aramaic idiom rather than a natural Greek phrase — its oddness in Greek is itself a clue that it's a title being carried over from a Semitic source, not native Greek theological vocabulary. |

The Greek's definiteness (ὁ) is precisely what the underlying Aramaic and the Aramaic-influenced Greek of Daniel's own Old Greek and Theodotion versions lack in the same fixed way. By the time the phrase reaches the Gospels, it has hardened from a description ("one like a son of man") into a title ("the Son of Man") — and Jesus consistently pairs it with Daniel's own imagery of clouds, glory, and universal dominion (e.g., Mark 13:26, Mark 14:62).

## Context & Background

**Literary setting.** Daniel 7 opens the second half of the book (chapters 7–12), which shifts from third-person court narratives (chapters 1–6) to first-person apocalyptic visions. Daniel 7 is written in Aramaic (the language continues from 2:4b through the end of chapter 7, then Hebrew resumes in chapter 8) — a linguistic seam scholars have long noted, per John J. Collins, *Daniel* (Hermeneia, 1993).

**Historical setting of the vision's content.** The four beasts are widely read as representing a sequence of empires — commonly identified (with variation among interpreters) as Babylon, Media, Persia, and Greece, culminating in the "little horn" arising from the fourth beast. John Goldingay, *Daniel* (Word Biblical Commentary, 1989), and John J. Collins, *Daniel* (Hermeneia, 1993), both discuss the "little horn" as most plausibly pointing to Antiochus IV Epiphanes (reigned 175–164 BC), whose persecution of Jewish religious practice (including desecration of the Jerusalem temple, 167 BC) is well-documented in 1 Maccabees and Josephus.

**Ancient Near Eastern / comparative context.** The imagery of a divine assembly, thrones set up for judgment, and "one like a son of man" riding on clouds has been compared by scholars to Canaanite mythological imagery — notably Ugaritic texts in which Baal is called "rider of the clouds" (an epithet applied to the storm-god). John Day, *God's Conflict with the Dragon and the Sea* (1985), and John J. Collins (Hermeneia, 1993) discuss how Daniel 7 may adapt older ANE combat-and-kingship mythology into a monotheistic, historicized apocalyptic vision — the cloud-rider language, applied to a human-like figure rather than a storm-god, is a significant transformation, not a straight borrowing.

**Geography/setting of the narrative frame.** The book situates Daniel in the Babylonian and Persian courts (Babylon under Nebuchadnezzar and Belshazzar, then the Medo-Persian administration), with chapter 7 dated by its own internal notice to "the first year of Belshazzar king of Babylon" (Daniel 7:1).

## The Dating Debate

This is a genuinely contested historical-critical question, and I'll state the positions plainly with names attached rather than pretend a false consensus either way.

**Sixth-century (traditional) dating.** This view holds Daniel was substantially written by a 6th-century BC figure during or shortly after the Babylonian exile, with the visions functioning as genuine predictive prophecy extending forward to the Hellenistic and beyond. Defended by scholars such as Gleason Archer, *Daniel* (Expositor's Bible Commentary, 1985), and Joyce Baldwin, *Daniel* (Tyndale Old Testament Commentaries, 1978), who point to Jesus' own apparent attribution of the book to "Daniel the prophet" (Matthew 24:15), the detailed knowledge of 6th-century Babylonian court life, and Ezekiel's mention of a "Daniel" as an ancient righteous figure (Ezekiel 14:14, 20) as supporting an early figure behind the tradition.

**Second-century (Maccabean-era) dating.** This view, associated with critical scholarship going back to the philosopher Porphyry (3rd century AD, per later citation in Jerome's commentary on Daniel) and argued in modern critical scholarship by John J. Collins, *Daniel* (Hermeneia, 1993), and John Goldingay, *Daniel* (Word Biblical Commentary, 1989), holds that the visions in chapters 7–12 were composed around 167–164 BC, during the persecution under Antiochus IV Epiphanes, on the grounds that the "predictions" track Hellenistic history in specific detail up through Antiochus but grow vague and inaccurate regarding his death (Daniel 11:40–45), suggesting the author wrote up to his own present and then genuinely predicted (inaccurately, on this view) what lay just beyond it. Collins and Goldingay also point to linguistic features (the Aramaic and the Persian/Greek loanwords) as consistent with a later date.

**Where the disagreement actually lives.** The dispute is not primarily textual but philosophical/methodological — it turns substantially on whether one's interpretive framework allows for genuine predictive prophecy centuries in advance, and on how the linguistic and historical evidence is weighed. Conservative scholars like Baldwin and Archer argue the Aramaic and loanword evidence is compatible with an earlier date properly understood; critical scholars like Collins and Goldingay read the same linguistic data as pointing later. This is a place where readers should expect their commentaries to disagree sharply, and I'd encourage consulting Collins (Hermeneia) and Goldingay (WBC) for the critical case, and Baldwin (Tyndale) or Archer (EBC) for the traditional case, to see the arguments in full rather than a summary alone.

## Interpretive Options

**Who is "one like a son of man" in Daniel 7 itself?**

1. **An individual heavenly/angelic figure**, possibly the archangel Michael or a messianic figure distinct from "the saints of the Most High." This reading notes that the figure is *given* dominion by the Ancient of Days in a scene distinct from the saints who *receive* the kingdom in 7:18, 22, 27 — suggesting the son of man mediates or represents them rather than simply being a symbol for them. Collins (Hermeneia, 1993) surveys this position alongside its history.

2. **A corporate symbol for "the saints of the Most High"** — Israel, or the faithful remnant, personified the way the beasts personify kingdoms. This reading leans on Daniel 7:18 and 7:27, where the kingdom is explicitly given to "the saints of the Most High." Goldingay (WBC, 1989) discusses this collective reading as one live option in the text's own logic.

3. **Both/either — an individual who represents a collective**, in keeping with common ANE and biblical patterns where a king or figure embodies a people (compare how the "servant" in Isaiah sometimes reads individually and sometimes corporately). Many commentators hold the ambiguity may be intentional, allowing later interpreters (including the New Testament writers) to develop the individual, messianic reading.

**How does the New Testament take up the title?**

Jesus' use of "Son of Man" as his most frequent self-designation, and especially his direct quotation of Daniel 7's imagery at his trial (Matthew 26:64; Mark 14:62), reads Daniel's figure individually and messianically — applying to himself the reception of everlasting dominion, glory, and a kingdom "that will never be destroyed," and explicitly forecasting his own future coming "on the clouds of heaven." This is a possible interpretation with strong textual grounding, not merely one option among equals: the verbal and conceptual overlap between Daniel 7:13–14 and Jesus' trial statement is close enough that most Gospels scholars across critical and confessional lines recognize a direct allusion, even where they differ on how self-consciously "titular" the phrase was in Jesus' own Aramaic speech versus how it was later crystallized by the Evangelists. See Darrell Bock, *Blasphemy and Exaltation in Judaism* (1998), for detailed argument on the trial scene's dependence on Daniel 7.
