## Direct Answer

In John 1:1c, "καὶ θεὸς ἦν ὁ λόγος" ("and the Word was God"), θεὸς appears **without the definite article** (anarthrous), unlike its two prior appearances in the verse where θεόν and ὁ θεὸς are articular. This grammatical fact has generated substantial scholarly discussion. The mainstream Trinitarian reading — represented by scholars like Daniel Wallace, C.K. Barrett, and D.A. Carson — holds that the anarthrous θεὸς functions as a qualitative predicate nominative, emphasizing the *nature* or *essence* of the Word (fully divine) rather than identifying the Word as "a god" distinct from the Father, or collapsing the Word into being identical with "the God" (ὁ θεός = the Father) mentioned in the previous clause. Jehovah's Witnesses' New World Translation and some others have rendered it "a god," a minority position rejected by the overwhelming weight of Greek grammarians.

## Original-Language Notes

The whole question turns on one word — θεὸς in the third clause — because its lack of the article, set against the articular ὁ θεός just before it, is precisely what generates the interpretive debate; λόγος and the article ὁ are worth a brief note only because they clarify why grammarians read θεὸς as they do.

### θεὸς (theos)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | John 1:1 — "the Word was God" |
| Lemma | θεός |
| Parsing | Noun, nominative singular masculine, predicate nominative (anarthrous) |
| Lexicon refs | BDAG 1; Louw-Nida 12.1 |
| Sense here | Divine nature/essence; qualitative, not indefinite ("a god") nor simply identical to ὁ θεός |
| Range of meaning | God (the one true God); a god/deity (pagan usage); divine being |
| Semantic domain | Louw-Nida Domain 12 (Supernatural Beings and Powers) |
| Etymology | Etymology alone cannot settle the sense here — the anarthrous form's function must be determined from Greek grammar and usage, not word-origin. |

This is the crux of the verse. Since Greek lacks an indefinite article ("a/an"), the presence or absence of the definite article ὁ carries interpretive weight that English cannot directly replicate. Daniel Wallace, in his *Greek Grammar Beyond the Basics* (1996), argues that anarthrous pre-verbal predicate nominatives in Greek are commonly **qualitative** — describing the nature of the subject — following the pattern identified by E.C. Colwell's 1933 rule (that definite predicate nouns preceding the verb often drop the article) but refined further by Wallace and Philip Harner (1973) to argue the emphasis here is qualitative rather than merely definite. On this reading, John is saying the Word possesses the same divine nature as ὁ θεός (the Father) without being personally identical to Him — avoiding both tritheism-adjacent readings and modalism.

### ὁ θεός (ho theos) — comparative note

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | John 1:1 — "the Word was with God" |
| Lemma | θεός (with article ὁ) |
| Parsing | Noun, accusative singular masculine (πρὸς τὸν θεόν), articular |
| Lexicon refs | BDAG 1 |
| Sense here | Refers specifically to God the Father as a distinct person |
| Range of meaning | Same as above — the article here marks definite personal reference |
| Semantic domain | Louw-Nida Domain 12 |
| Etymology | Same etymological caveat applies. |

The article's presence here (and its absence three words later) is the entire engine of the debate. Most grammarians, including C.K. Barrett (*The Gospel According to St John*, 1978) and D.A. Carson (*The Gospel According to John*, Pillar New Testament Commentary, 1991), read the shift as intentional: the articular ὁ θεός in clause two designates the person of the Father, while the anarthrous θεὸς in clause three characterizes the Word's nature as fully divine — the same essence, a distinct person.

## Context & Background

**Literary setting.** John 1:1–18 forms the Johannine Prologue, widely recognized by scholars such as Raymond Brown (*The Gospel According to John I–XII*, Anchor Bible, 1966) as a distinct poetic/hymnic unit that introduces the Gospel's Christology before the narrative begins. The triadic structure of verse 1 — three clauses, each building on the last — is itself a literary device many interpreters see as deliberate: it moves from the Word's eternal existence, to relational distinction (with God), to shared essence (was God).

**Greco-Roman and Hellenistic Jewish background.** The term λόγος carried rich resonance in the first-century Mediterranean world. In Stoic philosophy, λόγος denoted the rational principle ordering the universe. In Hellenistic Judaism, particularly in Philo of Alexandria (c. 20 BC–c. 50 AD), λόγος functioned as an intermediary figure between God and creation, drawing on personified Wisdom traditions (cf. Proverbs 8, Wisdom of Solomon 7). Craig Keener (*The Gospel of John: A Commentary*, 2003) notes that John's readers — whether more shaped by Jewish wisdom theology or Greek philosophical categories — would have recognized λόγος as pointing to a mediating divine agent, which John then identifies with Jesus in verse 14.

**Jewish monotheism as backdrop.** John writes within a strictly monotheistic Jewish context (cf. Deuteronomy 6:4). The theological tension the Prologue navigates — affirming the Word's full deity while maintaining a real distinction from "God" (the Father) — reflects the early Christian struggle to articulate Christ's identity without abandoning monotheism, a tension later Trinitarian doctrine sought to resolve dogmatically at Nicaea (325) and Constantinople (381).

## Interpretive Options

1. **Qualitative reading (mainstream Trinitarian view).** The anarthrous θεὸς describes the *nature* of the Word — fully divine, same essence as the Father, yet a distinct person. Advocates: Daniel Wallace (*Greek Grammar Beyond the Basics*, 1996), Philip Harner ("Qualitative Anarthrous Predicate Nouns," *Journal of Biblical Literature*, 1973), D.A. Carson (*The Gospel According to John*, Pillar New Testament Commentary, 1991), C.K. Barrett (*The Gospel According to St John*, 1978).

2. **Definite reading via Colwell's Rule.** E.C. Colwell's original 1933 study ("A Definite Rule for the Use of the Article in the Greek New Testament," *Journal of Biblical Literature*) argued that definite predicate nouns preceding the copula often lack the article simply due to word order, implying θεὸς here could still be fully definite ("the Word was *the* God") without collapsing into modalism, since the earlier clause already distinguishes the Word from ὁ θεός. Many who cite Colwell use it to support full deity while Wallace later refined the rule to argue for qualitative force specifically, seeing Colwell's rule as necessary but not sufficient to establish definiteness.

3. **Indefinite reading ("a god").** Adopted by the Jehovah's Witnesses' *New World Translation* and associated with anti-Trinitarian theology (e.g., some Arian-adjacent or unitarian positions). This reading treats the anarthrous θεὸς as indicating a lesser, distinct divine being. This is a minority position among Greek grammarians; Wallace, Harner, Barrett, Carson, and the substantial majority of published NT Greek grammars explicitly reject this rendering as grammatically unsupported, since anarthrous pre-copulative predicate nouns in Greek do not function like the English indefinite article.

4. **Historical patristic reading.** The Greek Fathers, writing as native or near-native speakers of Koine and later Attic-influenced Greek, uniformly read John 1:1c as affirming the full deity of the Word without indefiniteness — a reading foundational to Nicene Christology. Athanasius of Alexandria (c. 296–373) and the broader pro-Nicene tradition appealed to this verse against Arius's subordinationist Christology.
