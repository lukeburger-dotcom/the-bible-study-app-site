## Direct Answer

Genesis 1 shares a broad conceptual world with other ancient Near Eastern (ANE) creation texts — most famously the Babylonian *Enuma Elish* — including a watery pre-creation state, the separation of waters, the ordering of cosmic space, and the creation of humanity near the climax of the account. But the differences are sharper than the similarities: Genesis 1 has no theogony (no gods being born), no cosmic battle between deities, one sovereign God who speaks creation into being rather than fighting for it, and humanity created as the pinnacle of creation in God's "image" rather than as an afterthought to relieve the gods of labor. Scholars broadly agree Genesis 1 is written in conscious dialogue with this shared ANE cosmological vocabulary, but as a polemical and theological corrective to it — not a copy of it. This is a general view, represented by scholars like John Walton, Kenneth Kitchen, Gordon Wenham, and Bill Arnold, though they differ on how directly Genesis depends on specific Mesopotamian texts versus a shared cultural repertoire.

## Original-Language Notes

I've chosen **tehom** and **selem/demut**, because these two terms sit exactly where Genesis most visibly engages — and diverges from — its ANE environment; other vocabulary in the chapter is comparatively unremarkable in translation.

### תְּהוֹם (tehom)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Genesis 1:2 — "the deep" |
| Lemma | תְּהוֹם (tehom) |
| Parsing | Noun, feminine singular |
| Lexicon refs | BDB 1062; HALOT 1690 |
| Sense here | the primeval cosmic ocean/abyss preceding ordered creation |
| Range of meaning | Deep waters, the sea, subterranean waters, the primeval flood-abyss |
| Semantic domain | Cosmology / natural phenomena — water and chaos-imagery |
| Etymology | Long related by older scholarship to Akkadian *Tiamat*, the sea-goddess of *Enuma Elish*; this is now debated as a direct linguistic borrowing (see Context below) — etymology alone cannot settle the meaning, since a word's usage in its own context governs sense, not its supposed root or cognate. |

*Tehom* is the word most directly implicated in comparative discussion. Unlike Tiamat, *tehom* in Genesis 1 is not a deity or personal being to be defeated — it is inert material acted upon by God's word. Bill Arnold, *Genesis* (New Cambridge Bible Commentary, 2009), notes the term's likely etymological kinship with *Tiamat* while stressing Genesis strips it of any personhood or divinity.

### צֶלֶם / דְּמוּת (selem / demut)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Genesis 1:26 — "in Our image, according to Our likeness" |
| Lemma | צֶלֶם (selem); דְּמוּת (demut) |
| Parsing | Nouns, masculine singular (selem); feminine singular (demut) |
| Lexicon refs | BDB 853 (selem); BDB 198 (demut); HALOT 1028, 226 |
| Sense here | humanity made to represent/resemble God, functioning as his representative on earth |
| Range of meaning | selem: physical image, statue, representative figure; demut: likeness, resemblance, pattern |
| Semantic domain | Representation and royal/cultic ideology — image-bearing, sonship, representative rule |
| Etymology | selem is related to words for carved/cast images (idols) elsewhere in the Old Testament; this background illuminates but does not define its sense here — the text's own usage, applied to all humankind rather than a king or a cult statue, is decisive. |

In ANE royal ideology, kings alone were sometimes called the "image" of a god, and cult statues housed a deity's presence in a temple. John Walton, *Genesis* (NIV Application Commentary, 2001) and *The Lost World of Genesis One* (2009), argues Genesis 1 democratizes this royal/cultic language — every human, not just the king, bears God's image and rules as his representative.

## Context & Background

**The comparative texts.** The most-cited parallel is *Enuma Elish*, a Babylonian creation epic, extant in copies from the first millennium BC recovered in the library of Ashurbanipal at Nineveh (7th century BC), though many scholars date the composition itself earlier, into the second millennium BC. This is a general dating summary, not a specific citation. In it, the god Marduk defeats the sea-goddess Tiamat in combat, splits her carcass to form sky and earth, and humanity is created afterward from the blood of a slain god, Kingu, to do the gods' menial labor so the gods can rest. Other relevant texts include the Sumerian *Enki and Ninmah*, the Babylonian *Atrahasis* epic (which also has a flood account paired with a creation-of-humanity narrative), and Egyptian cosmologies such as the Memphite Theology, in which the god Ptah creates by speech — a point often raised alongside Genesis 1's creation-by-word. Kenneth Kitchen, *On the Reliability of the Old Testament* (2003), surveys these Egyptian and Mesopotamian materials and cautions against over-reading any single text as Genesis's direct "source."

**Real parallels, carefully stated.**
- A watery, undifferentiated state prior to ordered creation (Genesis 1:2; *Enuma Elish* Tablet I).
- Separation of upper and lower waters by a firmament/sky (Genesis 1:6–7 parallels Marduk's splitting of Tiamat to form the sky-dome).
- Creation proceeding in ordered stages culminating in humanity.
- Divine rest following creative work (Genesis 2:2–3; the gods' rest in *Enuma Elish* and *Atrahasis* after humanity is made to serve them).

Gordon Wenham, *Genesis 1–15* (Word Biblical Commentary, 1987), discusses these points of contact while stressing that Genesis reorders and reworks them theologically at nearly every turn.

**Real differences, carefully stated.**
- No theogony: Genesis's God has no origin story and no divine genealogy, unlike Marduk, Tiamat, or Apsu.
- No theomachy (divine combat): God's word, not violent conflict, accomplishes creation. Genesis 1 shows no battle motif at all — the closest ANE combat-myth echoes some scholars find are in poetic texts like Psalm 74:13–14 and Isaiah 51:9–10, which do use Leviathan/Rahab imagery, but Genesis 1 itself contains none of it.
- Humanity's status: rather than being created as an afterthought from a slain god's blood to relieve divine drudgery (*Enuma Elish*; *Atrahasis*), humanity in Genesis 1 is the crowning act of creation, made in God's own image, given dominion (Genesis 1:26–28).
- Genesis 1 presents strict monotheism against the polytheistic pantheons of its neighbors — sun and moon are not named or deified but called simply "the greater light" and "the lesser light" (Genesis 1:16), which John Walton and others read as a deliberate demotion of deities the Babylonians and Egyptians worshiped as gods (Shamash, the sun-god; Sin, the moon-god).

**Genre and purpose.** Bill Arnold and John Walton both argue Genesis 1 functions in a "temple-building" idiom common in the ANE, where a deity's ordering of the cosmos culminates in that deity taking up rest/rule in a temple — with the seventh day functioning as God "resting" (enthroning himself) over his cosmic temple. This reading (Walton's "cosmic temple inauguration" view) is one proposal among others, not a settled consensus.

## Interpretive Options

1. **Direct literary dependence** — Genesis borrows and polemically rewrites *Enuma Elish* specifically. This older view (associated with Hermann Gunkel in the early 20th century and popularized in the mid-20th century by Alexander Heidel, *The Babylonian Genesis*, 1942/1951) has waned as more texts have shown the shared motifs are widespread across ANE literature, not unique to Babylon.

2. **Shared ANE cosmological "commons," not direct borrowing** — Genesis draws on a broad, common ancient Near Eastern conceptual vocabulary (chaos-waters, ordered cosmos, divine rest) available across Mesopotamian, Egyptian, and Canaanite cultures, deliberately reshaping it to assert Yahweh's exclusive sovereignty. This is the position of Kenneth Kitchen and, with variations, John Walton and Gordon Wenham.

3. **Deliberate theological polemic** — Genesis 1 is best read as an intentional counter-narrative confronting the theology (not merely the literary form) of surrounding nations — asserting one God, no combat, no theogony, humans as royal image-bearers rather than slave labor. This is argued by scholars including Walton and, from a more traditionally conservative angle, Kitchen.

4. **Independent, non-comparative theological reading** — some interpreters (particularly within confessional traditions prior to and apart from 19th–20th century Assyriology) read Genesis 1 primarily on its own terms as revealed Scripture, without treating ANE parallels as determinative for interpretation, while not necessarily denying the shared cultural context existed.

These options are not mutually exclusive; most contemporary commentators (Wenham, Arnold, Walton) combine elements of 2 and 3.
