## Direct Answer

James 2:14–26 argues that genuine faith inevitably produces works, and that a "faith" without works is dead — unable to save. James is not contradicting Paul's teaching that justification is by faith apart from works of the law (Romans 3:28); rather, the two are addressing different questions and different opponents. Paul confronts those trusting in law-keeping (especially circumcision and Torah observance) as the ground of righteousness before God. James confronts those claiming an intellectual assent to monotheism ("faith") while showing no change of life. Martin Luther (1483–1546) famously struggled with this letter, calling it in his 1522 preface to the New Testament "an epistle of straw" compared to the Gospels and Paul's letters — though he did not remove it from his Bible and continued to cite it.

## Original-Language Notes

Two words carry the weight of this passage: **ἔργα** (works), which both James and Paul use but pointed at different targets, and **δικαιόω** (justified), the verb both writers share yet apply to different questions.

### δικαιόω (dikaioō)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | James 2:24 — "justified" |
| Lemma | δικαιόω |
| Parsing | verb, present passive indicative, 3rd singular |
| Lexicon refs | BDAG 1; Louw-Nida 34.5 |
| Sense here | to be shown righteous, vindicated, demonstrated as just — by evidence |

BDAG notes δικαιόω can mean "to render a favorable verdict" or "to demonstrate/show to be righteous." Many interpreters (e.g., Douglas Moo, *James*, Pillar New Testament Commentary, 2000) argue James uses it in the sense of vindication before others/at the final judgment — proving faith real — while Paul (Romans 3–4) uses it for the initial forensic declaration of righteousness before God apart from works. Same verb, different moment and audience in view.

### ἔργα (erga)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | James 2:24 — "works" |
| Lemma | ἔργον |
| Parsing | noun, neuter plural |
| Lexicon refs | BDAG 2; Louw-Nida 42.7 |
| Sense here | deeds/actions that embody and evidence one's faith |

For Paul in Galatians 2:16 and Romans 3:28, "works of the law" (ἔργα νόμου) typically denotes Torah-observance (circumcision, food laws, Sabbath) as a boundary marker of covenant membership — a phrase James never uses. James's "works" are concrete acts of mercy (2:15–16) and obedience (Abraham's offering of Isaac, 2:21). This distinction is central to how scholars like James Dunn ("The New Perspective on Paul") and Moo reconcile the two writers.

## Context & Background

James, traditionally identified with James the brother of Jesus and leader of the Jerusalem church (cf. Acts 15; Galatians 1:19), writes to Jewish Christians scattered outside Palestine (James 1:1), likely addressing a community where an abstract or merely verbal "faith" — assent to the Shema ("God is one," James 2:19) — had become detached from ethical practice. Douglas Moo (*James*, Pillar New Testament Commentary, 2000) notes the letter reflects Jewish wisdom tradition, paralleling concerns found in Proverbs and the Sermon on the Mount about the gap between profession and practice.

Whether James is written before, during, or in response to a distorted version of Paul's teaching is debated. Moo and others date James early (mid-40s CE, before the Jerusalem Council), which would mean James cannot be directly countering Paul's letters (written later) but may be countering a slogan-level misuse of "faith alone" already circulating. Others, such as Luke Timothy Johnson (*The Letter of James*, Anchor Bible, 1995), see James addressing a broader ethical concern independent of any direct engagement with Pauline theology.

## Interpretive Options

1. **Complementary, not contradictory** (Moo, Johnson, most evangelical and Catholic commentators): James and Paul answer different questions — Paul: "How is a sinner declared righteous before God?" (by faith, apart from works of law); James: "What does genuine, living faith look like?" (it produces works). This is a general view, not a specific citation to any single consensus statement, though it is widely represented across Moo and Johnson's commentaries.

2. **Luther's tension**: Martin Luther, in his 1522 "Preface to the New Testament," questioned James's apostolic authority and placed it at the end of his German New Testament alongside Hebrews, Jude, and Revelation, famously calling it "an epistle of straw" ("stroherne Epistel") because "it has nothing of the nature of the gospel about it." Luther's concern was that James 2:24, read in isolation, seemed to teach justification by works, which he saw as undermining the gospel of grace. Notably, Luther did not remove James from the canon and continued to quote it elsewhere; his judgment reflects a hierarchy of esteem among New Testament books rather than outright rejection (see Luther's Works, American Edition, vol. 35).

3. **Catholic/Reformed engagement**: The Council of Trent (1545–1563) cited James 2 in defense of the necessity of works flowing from justifying grace. Reformed theologians (e.g., John Calvin, *Commentary on James*) argued James is describing the *fruit* that inevitably accompanies true faith, not another *ground* of justification — works as the evidence, not the instrument.
