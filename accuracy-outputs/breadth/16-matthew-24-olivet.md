## Direct Answer

Matthew 24 records Jesus' response to His disciples' questions about the destruction of the temple and the "sign" of His coming and the end of the age. Three major interpretive frameworks read this chapter differently: **preterism** (the discourse primarily or entirely concerns the fall of Jerusalem in AD 70), **futurism** (the discourse primarily or entirely concerns end-time events yet to come), and **mixed/partial-preterist** readings (the discourse addresses both AD 70 and a still-future return of Christ, with a transition point somewhere in the chapter — most commonly at verse 36). This is a genuinely contested question among faithful interpreters, and the strongest arguments on each side turn heavily on how one reads the disciples' compound question in verse 3, the referent of "this generation" in verse 34, and the meaning of "immediately" in verse 29.

## Original-Language Notes

The words worth pausing on are *genea* ("generation," v. 34) and *parousia* ("coming," v. 3, 27, 37, 39) — the entire interpretive debate largely hinges on these two terms, since English "generation" and "coming" carry assumptions the Greek doesn't automatically settle.

### γενεά (genea)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Matthew 24:34 — "this generation" |
| Lemma | γενεά |
| Parsing | noun, nominative singular feminine |
| Lexicon refs | BDAG; Louw-Nida 10.36–10.40 |
| Sense here | a body of people living at the same time; contemporaries |
| Range of meaning | can denote (1) a group of contemporaries/those living at a given time, (2) a generation as a unit of time (roughly 30–40 years), or (3) in some contexts, a race/kind of people (an "ethnic" or moral-character sense, as some argue for Matthew 23:36) |
| Semantic domain | kinship/time; groups defined by descent or shared era |
| Etymology | related to γίνομαι ("to become, be born"); etymology alone does not settle the sense — usage in context governs meaning, not word origin |

Preterists and mixed-view interpreters who place the "generation" cutoff before verse 36 argue *genea* here means Jesus' own contemporaries, matching its consistent use elsewhere in Matthew (e.g., Matthew 23:36, "all these things will come upon this generation"). Futurists who read the entire discourse as end-time argue for the less common "race" sense (i.e., the Jewish people as a whole will not pass away) or hold that "this generation" refers to a future generation that witnesses the events of verses 15–31. Both readings are represented among serious commentators; the "contemporaries" sense is the dominant usage of *genea* throughout Matthew's Gospel (D.A. Carson, *Matthew*, Expositor's Bible Commentary, 1984).

### παρουσία (parousia)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Matthew 24:3 — "Your coming" |
| Lemma | παρουσία |
| Parsing | noun, genitive singular feminine |
| Lexicon refs | BDAG; TDNT |
| Sense here | arrival, presence — used here of Christ's return |
| Range of meaning | ordinary sense: "arrival" or "presence" of a person (used of official visits of rulers/dignitaries in Hellenistic usage); in the New Testament epistles becomes a technical term for Christ's second coming |
| Semantic domain | presence, arrival, visitation |
| Etymology | from πάρειμι ("to be present"); the term's later technical theological usage should not be read backward into every occurrence without contextual warrant |

The disciples' question in verse 3 links "your *parousia*" with "the end of the age" as though these were one and the same event — a linkage some interpreters argue reflects the disciples' own (possibly mistaken) assumption that temple destruction and cosmic consummation were simultaneous. How one construes the disciples' assumption shapes the entire reading of Jesus' answer (R.T. France, *The Gospel of Matthew*, New International Commentary on the New Testament, 2007).

Want the word behind "sign" (σημεῖον) in verse 3, or "immediately" (εὐθέως) in verse 29? Just ask.

## Context & Background

**Setting and occasion.** Jesus has just pronounced judgment on Jerusalem and its religious leadership (Matthew 23), culminating in the lament "your house is left to you desolate" (Matthew 23:38). He then leaves the temple and predicts its total destruction (24:2). The disciples, walking with Him to the Mount of Olives — a hill east of Jerusalem overlooking the Temple Mount across the Kidron Valley — ask their compound question privately. Craig Keener notes that first-century Jewish hearers would have naturally assumed temple destruction and the end of the age were linked events, since the temple's standing was bound up with Israel's covenantal hopes (Craig Keener, *The Gospel of Matthew: A Socio-Rhetorical Commentary*, 2009).

**Historical background of AD 70.** The Roman siege and destruction of Jerusalem under Titus in AD 70, during the First Jewish-Roman War (AD 66–73), is documented by the Jewish historian Josephus (*The Jewish War*), who was an eyewitness. Josephus describes the temple's burning, the immense loss of life, and conditions of famine and siege matching much of the language in Matthew 24:15–21 (Josephus, *Jewish War*, Books 5–6). This historical event is the anchor point for preterist and mixed readings.

**"Abomination of desolation."** Matthew 24:15 invokes Daniel 9:27; 11:31; 12:11. In Second Temple Jewish memory, this phrase already had a referent: Antiochus IV Epiphanes' desecration of the temple in 167 BC (described in 1 Maccabees 1:54). Luke's parallel account interprets the sign more directly in terms of military siege: "when you see Jerusalem surrounded by armies, know that its desolation is near" (Luke 21:20) — a detail futurists and preterists both must account for in relating Luke's version to Matthew's.

**Fig tree and agricultural imagery.** The fig tree parable (24:32–33) draws on a common feature of Judean agriculture: the fig tree, unlike evergreens, visibly loses its leaves in winter and their return signals the nearness of summer — a natural, locally observable sign familiar to Jesus' audience (BDAG; general background in Kenneth Bailey, *Poet and Peasant/Through Peasant Eyes*, combined ed., 1983, on Palestinian agrarian imagery).

**Apocalyptic genre conventions.** Cosmic-collapse language ("the sun will be darkened... the stars will fall," 24:29) echoes Old Testament prophetic oracles against nations (Isaiah 13:10 against Babylon; Isaiah 34:4 against Edom; Ezekiel 32:7 against Egypt) — texts that use cosmic imagery to describe historical divine judgments on earthly powers, not necessarily the literal end of the physical universe. G.K. Beale and others argue this genre convention is essential background for reading Matthew 24:29 (G.K. Beale, *The Book of Revelation*, New International Greek Testament Commentary, 1999, discussing this convention broadly across apocalyptic literature).

## Interpretive Options

**1. Full Preterism** — the entire discourse, including the *parousia* language, was fulfilled in AD 70.
- Strongest argument: Jesus explicitly bounds the events by "this generation" (24:34), the most natural referent being His own contemporaries, and Luke's parallel (Luke 21:20–24) explicitly identifies the "abomination of desolation" with the Roman siege of Jerusalem.
- Representative advocates: R.C. Sproul explored this reading sympathetically in *The Last Days According to Jesus* (1998), though Sproul himself stopped short of full preterism regarding the final resurrection and second coming proper; consistent full-preterist positions (sometimes called "hyper-preterism") are associated with writers like Don Preston.
- Weakness often raised against it: verses like 24:30–31 (the Son of Man "coming on the clouds," gathering the elect "from the four winds") and 24:36 (no one knows "that day and hour," implying something beyond the predictable countdown to a historical siege) seem to some interpreters to strain against a purely local, historical fulfillment (D.A. Carson, *Matthew*, Expositor's Bible Commentary, 1984).

**2. Full Futurism** — the discourse (at least from v. 15 or so onward) describes end-time events still future to us, often tied to a future "great tribulation," a future antichrist figure, and a visible, cosmic second coming.
- Strongest argument: the scale of language in 24:21 ("there will be great distress, unequaled from the beginning of the world until now, and never to be equaled again") seems to exceed what happened in AD 70, since Jesus elsewhere anticipates history continuing (the Great Commission, judgment of the nations); and 24:29–31 describes cosmic disturbance and a visible, universal gathering of the elect that did not visibly occur in AD 70.
- Representative advocates: dispensational futurists such as John Walvoord (*Matthew: Thy Kingdom Come*, 1974) read Matthew 24:15–31 as end-time tribulation events, often correlated with Daniel's seventieth week and Revelation.
- Weakness often raised against it: it must either read "this generation" (v. 34) as referring to a distant future generation or reinterpret *genea* nonstandardly, which critics like Carson argue strains Matthew's consistent usage of the term elsewhere in his Gospel.

**3. Mixed / Partial-Preterist (transition) reading** — verses 4–35 (or similar range) refer to AD 70, while a transition occurs (commonly located at v. 36, "but concerning that day and hour") shifting to the still-future, final return of Christ.
- Strongest argument: Jesus' own language shifts noticeably at verse 36 — from datable, observable signs ("when you see," v. 15, 33) to explicit unknowability ("no one knows... not even the Son," v. 36), and from local geographic urgency (v. 16–20, fleeing Judea) to universal, unexpected judgment imagery (Noah, thief in the night, vv. 37–44). This shift is read as marking a genuine subject change.
- Representative advocates: this is a common reading among commentators including R.T. France (*The Gospel of Matthew*, NICNT, 2007) and, with variations, D.A. Carson (*Matthew*, EBC, 1984), both of whom see verses 4–35 addressed substantially to AD 70 (with Carson allowing some verses to carry double reference) and verses 36 onward turning to the unpredictable, future parousia.
- Weakness often raised against it: critics on both sides note the transition point is not marked by an explicit textual signal as clean as advocates suggest, and where exactly the shift occurs (v. 22, v. 29, v. 34, or v. 36) is itself disputed among mixed-view interpreters.

This is a genuinely contested question among careful, Bible-believing interpreters — it is not a matter the text settles unambiguously by itself, and naming the disagreement honestly is more faithful than manufacturing false certainty in either direction.
