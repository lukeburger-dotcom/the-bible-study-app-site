## Direct Answer

Song of Songs 4 is an extended love poem in which the bridegroom praises his bride's beauty from head to shoulders (vv. 1–7) and then invites her into intimacy, likening her to a garden and spring (vv. 8–16). Across church history it has been read three main ways: **allegorically** (as a picture of God/Christ and Israel/the Church), **typologically** (as pointing forward to Christ and the Church while preserving a real historical referent), and **naturally/literally** (as genuine erotic love poetry between husband and wife, celebrated in its own right as part of the created goodness of marriage). Each reading has serious representative advocates, and each carries real interpretive costs and payoffs.

## Original-Language Notes

I've chosen **גַּן נָעוּל (gan naʿul)** and **רֵעְיָתִי (raʿyati)** because the whole history of interpretation pivots on whether these images are read as literal-erotic, typological, or purely allegorical figures — and because the garden metaphor in v. 12 is the single most debated image in the chapter.

### גַּן נָעוּל (gan naʿul)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Song of Songs 4:12 — "a garden locked up" |
| Lemma | גַּן (gan) + נָעַל (naʿal) |
| Parsing | masculine singular noun + Qal passive participle |
| Lexicon refs | HALOT (גַּן); BDB 171 (גַּן), BDB 653 (נָעַל) |
| Sense here | an enclosed, cultivated garden; "locked" conveys exclusivity and untouched purity |
| Range of meaning | Literal garden/orchard (Gen 2:8; Eccl 2:5); figurative for a person or people set apart (Isa 51:3; Isa 58:11) |
| Semantic domain | Agriculture / horticulture; figuratively, sexuality and covenant exclusivity |
| Etymology | נָעַל relates to bolting or locking a door; the root itself does not "prove" a doctrine of chastity — the sense comes from context, not derivation (etymological fallacy caveat). |

This image is the crux: read literally, it is erotic praise of the bride's exclusive devotion to her husband before/within marriage. Read allegorically by Jewish and Christian interpreters, the "locked garden" becomes Israel's or the Church's set-apart holiness, or Mary's virginity in some patristic and medieval readings.

### רֵעְיָתִי (raʿyati)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Song of Songs 4:1 — "my darling" |
| Lemma | רַעְיָה (raʿyah), from רֵעַ (reaʿ) |
| Parsing | feminine singular noun with 1st person singular suffix |
| Lexicon refs | BDB 946; HALOT (רַעְיָה) |
| Sense here | "my companion," "my beloved" — a term of intimate partnership, not mere physical desire |
| Range of meaning | "Friend/companion" (masculine reaʿ used broadly in Hebrew); feminine form used only in Song of Songs for the beloved |
| Semantic domain | Relational/covenantal companionship; used here in an erotic-marital context |
| Etymology | Shares a root with the common word for "friend/neighbor," but Song of Songs uses the feminine form exclusively for the bride — don't read generic "friendship" back into it; context, not root, governs sense. |

This term matters because it frames the eroticism of the chapter within relational intimacy and companionship — a detail allegorical readers seize on for covenant-partner imagery (God/Israel, Christ/Church) and natural readers cite as evidence of real marital affection.

## Context & Background

**Genre and setting.** Song of Songs belongs to ancient Near Eastern love poetry, a genre well attested in Egyptian love songs of the New Kingdom period (c. 1300–1100 BC) that similarly use garden, animal, and body imagery to celebrate romantic and physical love — a comparison drawn out by Michael Fox, *The Song of Songs and the Ancient Egyptian Love Songs* (1985). The wasf (a poetic form praising a lover's body part by part) found in 4:1–7 has parallels in later Arabic wedding poetry, noted by Marvin Pope, *Song of Songs* (Anchor Bible, 1977).

**Imagery drawn from geography and agriculture.** The chapter's similes assume a Judean/Israelite agrarian landscape: Gilead (v. 1) was a hill region east of the Jordan known for pasturing goats; Lebanon (vv. 8, 11, 15) supplied cedar and was associated with fragrant resins and snow-capped heights; pomegranates, henna, nard, saffron, cinnamon, and frankincense (vv. 13–14) reflect a spice trade connecting Israel to Arabia and India, attested in ancient trade records discussed by Pope (1977). These were luxury imports, signaling opulence and sensory abundance in the poem's garden imagery.

**Marriage customs.** Some scholars, including Roland Murphy, *Song of Songs* (Hermeneia, 1990), note the chapter's language (bride, sister-bride pairing, garden entered by the beloved) coheres with ancient Near Eastern wedding-song conventions, though the Song's superscription and lack of narrative framework make a firm liturgical setting uncertain.

**Canonical history.** The book's inclusion in the Hebrew canon was debated among early rabbis; the Mishnah (*Yadayim* 3:5) records Rabbi Akiva (c. 50–c. 135) defending its sanctity, reportedly calling it the "Holy of Holies" among Scripture, which suggests both its contested status and its eventual acceptance via allegorical reading of Israel's covenant love for God.

## Interpretive Options

**1. Allegorical reading (Jewish and Christian traditions, patristic through medieval periods).**
Jewish tradition, reflected in the Targum on Song of Songs (dated by scholars to the early medieval period) and later rabbinic commentary, read the book as an allegory of God's love for Israel through covenant history. Christian allegorical reading was dominant from the early church through the Reformation: Origen (c. 185–c. 253) wrote a multi-volume commentary treating the Song as depicting Christ's love for the soul and the Church (his commentary survives partially in Latin translation by Rufinus). Bernard of Clairvaux (1090–1153) preached 86 sermons on the Song's opening chapters, reading the bride as the individual soul or the Church in mystical union with Christ. On this reading, the body-imagery of chapter 4 (eyes, hair, teeth, lips, the garden) is read symbolically — e.g., Bernard treats the garden as the soul's interior life cultivated by grace. **Strength:** honors centuries of devotional and mystical use, and offers a coherent theological payoff (union with God). **Weakness:** critics such as Fox (1985) and Tremper Longman III, *Song of Songs* (New International Commentary on the Old Testament, 2001), argue it often detaches from the plain sense and imposes meaning not signaled by the text itself.

**2. Typological reading (a mediating position).**
This view, articulated by scholars such as Duane Garrett, *Song of Songs* (Word Biblical Commentary, 2004), holds that the Song describes real marital love as its primary, historical sense, while also functioning — like other Old Testament marriage and covenant language (Hosea, Ezekiel 16, Isaiah 62) — as a type pointing toward Christ's love for the Church, made explicit in Ephesians 5:25–32 and Revelation 19:7–9. On this reading, chapter 4's celebration of the bride's beauty and the "garden locked" typifies the covenant faithfulness and future consummation between Christ and his people, without denying the human marriage in view. **Strength:** retains the literal historical/erotic sense while connecting the Song to the New Testament's own marriage imagery for Christ and the Church. **Weakness:** the typological connection is inferred from broader biblical theology rather than stated explicitly within the Song itself.

**3. Natural/literal reading (dominant in modern critical and much evangelical scholarship).**
This view reads Song of Songs 4 as straightforward, unapologetic celebration of erotic and marital love between a husband and wife, without symbolic overlay. Representative scholars include Fox (1985), Pope (1977), Murphy (1990), and Longman (2001), who argue the poem's ANE parallels, sensory vocabulary, and lack of any stated allegorical key point to a poem about human love as good and worth celebrating in itself — consistent with a "creation theology" reading of marriage rooted in Genesis 2:24. **Strength:** takes the genre and ANE literary parallels seriously and avoids imposing meaning the text doesn't signal. **Weakness:** on a purely naturalistic reading, some ask why such an intensely erotic book was included in the canon at all — a question the allegorical and typological readings answer more directly.
