## Direct Answer

In Exodus 3:14, when Moses asks for God's name, God responds with the Hebrew phrase *ehyeh asher ehyeh*, rendered "I AM WHO I AM" (BSB, KJV). This is one of the most theologically dense moments in the Hebrew Bible — God's self-identification is bound up with the verb "to be," linking his name (YHWH, revealed in the next verse) to his nature as the self-existent, ever-present, covenant-keeping God. The major interpretive options cluster around whether this is a statement of self-existence (ontological), a promise of active presence (relational/existential), or a deliberate refusal to be pinned down (evasive/majestic). This is a general summary of the interpretive landscape, not a single settled reading.

## Original-Language Notes

I chose **ehyeh** and **YHWH** because the entire passage turns on the relationship between the verb "to be" and the divine name that follows in verse 15 — the wordplay is the theological content, not decoration.

### אֶהְיֶה (ehyeh)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Exodus 3:14 — "I AM WHO I AM" |
| Lemma | היה (hayah) |
| Parsing | Qal imperfect, 1st person common singular |
| Lexicon refs | BDB 224–226; HALOT 244–246 |
| Sense here | "I am / I will be" — the imperfect aspect of the verb "to be," conveying ongoing or future existence/action |
| Range of meaning | To exist, to happen, to become, to be present, to prove to be |
| Semantic domain | Existence/state of being; also used in covenant-presence formulas ("I will be with you") |
| Etymology | The root היה is common Hebrew vocabulary for "to be/become"; its use as a name is theologically loaded, but the meaning here rests on grammar and context, not on breaking the word into hypothetical original roots. |

The Hebrew imperfect (*yiqtol*) can carry a present, future, or habitual-durative sense depending on context, which is precisely why translators and theologians disagree on whether God means "I am" (timeless existence) or "I will be" (ongoing faithful presence). Both are grammatically legitimate readings of the same form.

### יהוה (YHWH)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Exodus 3:15 — "the LORD" |
| Lemma | יהוה |
| Parsing | Proper noun (traditionally understood as related to a 3rd person form of היה) |
| Lexicon refs | BDB 217–218; HALOT 393–394 |
| Sense here | The personal covenant name of the God of Israel |
| Range of meaning | Used exclusively as the divine name; not applied to any other referent in the Hebrew Bible |
| Semantic domain | Proper name / divine title, distinct from common nouns for "God" (elohim, el) |
| Etymology | Widely connected by scholars to the verb היה ("to be"), making it a probable 3rd-person form ("he is" / "he causes to be") that echoes God's 1st-person self-declaration in verse 14 — but the precise derivation is debated and should not be treated as fully settled etymology. |

The link between *ehyeh* (1st person, "I am") and *YHWH* (likely 3rd person, "he is") is the interpretive key many scholars point to: God names himself in the first person to Moses, but the name Israel will use is the third-person form — as if "I AM" becomes, on Israel's lips, "HE IS."

## Context & Background

**Narrative setting.** Moses encounters God at "Horeb, the mountain of God" (Exodus 3:1, BSB) while shepherding Jethro's flock, in a scene involving a burning bush that is not consumed. According to Douglas Stuart, *Exodus* (New American Commentary, 2006), this location is presented as remote wilderness terrain, consistent with the semi-nomadic pastoral life of Moses during his years in Midian.

**Ancient Near Eastern naming conventions.** In the ancient Near East generally, to know a deity's name was understood as gaining a kind of access to or relationship with that deity — names were not mere labels but carried identity and power. This is a general view reflected in comparative ANE studies rather than a claim tied to one specific citation; Moses' question "What is His name?" (Exodus 3:13) reflects this broader cultural expectation that the Israelites would want assurance of exactly which god, and what kind of god, was sending Moses.

**The Tetragrammaton in later Jewish practice.** By the Second Temple period, Jewish tradition avoided pronouncing the name YHWH aloud, substituting "Adonai" ("my Lord") in reading and prayer — a practice reflected in the Masoretic vocalization tradition and standard in most English translations rendering YHWH as "the LORD" in small capitals. This is a well-documented historical-textual practice, described in standard reference works such as Bruce Waltke, *An Old Testament Theology* (2007).

**Geography.** Horeb/Sinai is traditionally located in the wilderness of the Sinai Peninsula, though the exact site is debated among scholars and not resolvable from the text alone; this is a general observation, not a settled geographic claim.

## Interpretive Options

Scholars and theological traditions have proposed several major readings of *ehyeh asher ehyeh*:

1. **Ontological/self-existence reading.** God is declaring his eternal, self-sufficient being — "I am the One who simply is," independent of all other causes. This reading has deep roots in patristic and medieval theology; it was reinforced historically by the Septuagint's rendering *egō eimi ho ōn* ("I am the one who is/exists"), which pushed the verse toward a philosophical, being-centered sense picked up later by thinkers like Augustine and Aquinas. Discussed in Brevard Childs, *The Book of Exodus* (Old Testament Library, 1974).

2. **Relational/presence reading ("I will be who I will be" / "I will be with you").** Many Hebrew Bible scholars, including Walter Brueggemann, *Exodus* (New Interpreter's Bible, 1994), and Terence Fretheim, *Exodus* (Interpretation, 1991), argue the imperfect verb form points forward — God is promising active, faithful presence with Israel through the coming exodus and beyond, echoing the assurance already given in verse 12: "I will be with you" (Exodus 3:12, BSB). On this view, the name is less about abstract metaphysics and more about covenant reliability in the unfolding story.

3. **Refusal/majestic-evasion reading.** Some interpreters, following the tradition represented in Martin Buber and Franz Rosenzweig's German translation of the Torah (20th century), read the repeated verb as God's way of declining to be fully named or controlled — "I will be what/whoever I will be" — asserting divine freedom and sovereignty over the encounter rather than handing Moses a definable label to invoke or manipulate, as ANE naming conventions might have expected.

4. **Combined/etymological reading tied to YHWH.** Many commentators, such as John Durham, *Exodus* (Word Biblical Commentary, 1987), connect the *ehyeh* wordplay directly to the divine name YHWH revealed in verse 15, treating verse 14 as an interpretive gloss explaining the name rather than a separate philosophical statement — the name itself carries the "being/becoming" sense embedded in the verb.

These readings are not always mutually exclusive; several commentators (e.g., Fretheim) hold that the ambiguity of the Hebrew imperfect may be intentional, carrying both the sense of being and the sense of active, promised presence simultaneously.
