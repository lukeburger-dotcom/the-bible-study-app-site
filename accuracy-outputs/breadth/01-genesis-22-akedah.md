## Direct Answer

Genesis 22 recounts God commanding Abraham to offer his son Isaac as a burnt offering, Abraham's unhesitating obedience up to the moment of the knife, and God's last-second provision of a ram in Isaac's place. The text explicitly frames this as a *test* (Genesis 22:1). What God is doing, on the plain sense of the narrative, is proving — not discovering for God's own sake, but demonstrating and confirming publicly — the authenticity of Abraham's fear of God and trust in God's promise-keeping character, even when the command seems to contradict the promise itself (that through Isaac Abraham's offspring would be reckoned, Genesis 21:12). The episode has generated the single richest body of interpretation in Genesis: Jewish tradition calls it the *Akedah* ("the binding") and reads it as the supreme act of Israel's founding merit and faithfulness; Christian tradition reads Isaac as a type of Christ and the ram as substitutionary provision, with the whole scene as gospel foreshadowing. Both readings are traceable to named interpreters across centuries, detailed below.

## Original-Language Notes

Three words carry the weight of this passage: נִסָּה (the "test" that frames the whole episode), יָחִיד ("only/beloved" son, which sharpens the cost), and the divine self-oath formula in verse 16 — the rest of the chapter's vocabulary is straightforward narrative Hebrew that the English renders adequately.

### נִסָּה (nissah)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Genesis 22:1 — "God tested Abraham" |
| Lemma | נָסָה (nasah) |
| Parsing | Verb, Piel perfect, 3rd masculine singular |
| Lexicon refs | BDB 650; HALOT 702 |
| Sense here | to test, try, prove (the genuineness or quality of something) |
| Range of meaning | Can mean "test/try" (of persons, as here), "attempt" (Deuteronomy 28:56), or "prove/tempt" in older English usage (KJV) |
| Semantic domain | Testing, proving, examining |
| Etymology | Possibly related to a root meaning "to lift up" or "to attempt," but etymology does not determine meaning here — the word's usage across the Hebrew Bible (e.g., Exodus 15:25, Deuteronomy 8:2) establishes the sense of divine testing of covenant faithfulness, not temptation to sin. |

The KJV's "tempt" reflects older English usage where "tempt" meant "test," not "entice to sin" — a point commentators regularly flag to prevent misreading, since James 1:13 states God tempts no one to evil. Nahum Sarna, *Genesis* (JPS Torah Commentary, 1989), notes that nissah in the Hebrew Bible consistently denotes a trial that reveals character already present, not an experiment to discover the unknown.

### יָחִיד (yachid)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Genesis 22:2 — "your only son" |
| Lemma | יָחִיד (yachid) |
| Parsing | Adjective, masculine singular |
| Lexicon refs | BDB 402; HALOT 405 |
| Sense here | only one, unique, precious — the sole remaining son of promise |
| Range of meaning | "Only child" (Judges 11:34), "solitary/lonely" (Psalm 25:16), or by extension "beloved, precious" |
| Semantic domain | Uniqueness, singularity, preciousness |
| Etymology | From a root denoting "oneness/unity"; the etymological link to "one" is real but the theological weight comes from usage and context (Ishmael was also Abraham's son but excluded from the promise), not from the word's root alone. |

Ishmael still lived, so "only son" cannot mean Abraham's sole offspring numerically — it marks Isaac as the unique son of the covenant promise, which is precisely why the command in verse 2 is so devastating: God asks Abraham to sacrifice the very son through whom the promise of Genesis 21:12 was to be fulfilled.

### נִשְׁבַּעְתִּי (nishba'ti) — the divine oath

This is the only place in Genesis where God swears "by Myself" — the author of Hebrews (6:13) later singles out this self-oath as the ultimate ground of covenantal certainty, since God "could swear by no one greater."

## Context & Background

**Geography.** The "land of Moriah" (Genesis 22:2) is mentioned by name only here and in 2 Chronicles 3:1, where Solomon builds the temple "on Mount Moriah... where the LORD had appeared to his father David." The identification of Moriah with the Jerusalem temple mount is a Jewish and Christian interpretive tradition traceable at least to the Chronicler (2 Chronicles 3:1) and affirmed by Josephus, *Antiquities of the Jews* 1.13.2 (1st century). The three-day journey (Genesis 22:4) fits a distance consistent with travel from Beersheba toward the Jerusalem highlands, as noted by Gordon Wenham, *Genesis 16–50* (Word Biblical Commentary, 1994).

**Cultural setting: child sacrifice in the ancient Near East.** Human sacrifice, including child sacrifice, is attested in the wider ancient Near Eastern and Levantine world, though its extent and frequency are debated among archaeologists. The Hebrew Bible elsewhere explicitly condemns child sacrifice to Molech (Leviticus 18:21; 20:2–5; 2 Kings 23:10) and treats it as a Canaanite practice Israel must not imitate. This background sharpens the horror of God's initial command in Genesis 22:2 — Abraham, coming out of a Mesopotamian and then Canaanite cultural milieu where such sacrifice was not unthinkable, receives a command that would not have seemed as self-evidently unthinkable to him as it does to modern readers, per John Walton, *Genesis* (NIV Application Commentary, 2001), who notes the narrative's function partly as a decisive rejection of child sacrifice even while using its idiom.

**Material culture.** The "burnt offering" (olah, Genesis 22:2, 22:13) is the standard whole-offering of Israelite sacrificial practice, entirely consumed on the altar (cf. Leviticus 1). The ram caught by its horns "in a thicket" reflects the scrub vegetation of the Judean hill country. Wood for the offering, a knife, and fire are the standard implements Abraham carries (Genesis 22:6), consistent with ordinary sacrificial practice of the patriarchal period as reconstructed from comparative Near Eastern and biblical evidence.

**Historical-critical note.** Documentary source critics have traditionally assigned Genesis 22 to the Elohist (E) source, based on the divine name Elohim in verses 1, 3, 8, 9 shifting to "the angel of the LORD" (YHWH) in verses 11, 14 — a classic case cited in discussions of pentateuchal sources; see Richard Elliott Friedman, *Who Wrote the Bible?* (1987), for a representative documentary-hypothesis treatment. This is a critical-scholarly reconstruction, not a claim inherent to the text itself.

## Interpretive Options

**1. Jewish tradition: the Akedah as Israel's foundational merit.** Rabbinic literature elevates the Akedah as the supreme demonstration of faith, frequently linked to Israel's ongoing covenant standing. The *Targum Pseudo-Jonathan* and later midrashim (e.g., *Genesis Rabbah* 56, compiled c. 5th–6th century) develop the tradition that Isaac was a willing adult participant, not merely a passive victim — some rabbinic sources imagine Isaac as around 37 years old, making his cooperation itself an act of heroic faith equal to his father's. The concept of *zekhut avot* ("merit of the fathers") ties the Akedah to Israel's later deliverance; the ram's horn becomes associated with the shofar blown at Rosh Hashanah, when the Akedah is the traditional Torah reading, as documented by Jon Levenson, *The Death and Resurrection of the Beloved Son* (1993), a major study of the Akedah's reception history.

**2. Jewish tradition: Kierkegaard's philosophical reading (Christian, but shaping later Jewish-Christian dialogue).** Søren Kierkegaard, *Fear and Trembling* (1843), reads Abraham as the paradigmatic "knight of faith" who undergoes a "teleological suspension of the ethical" — obeying God even against the universal ethical demand not to kill one's child. This reading, while by a Christian philosopher, has become a major touchstone across both Jewish and Christian philosophical engagement with the text.

**3. Christian typological reading: Isaac as a type of Christ.** From the early church onward, Isaac carrying the wood for his own sacrifice (Genesis 22:6) is read as prefiguring Christ carrying his own cross; the substitutionary ram is read as pointing to the substitutionary death of Christ. This typology appears in the church fathers — Melito of Sardis, *On Pascha* (2nd century), and later Augustine, *City of God* 16.32 (early 5th century), both draw this connection. Hebrews 11:17–19 in the New Testament itself already reads the episode as Abraham reasoning "that God could raise the dead," making Isaac's return a type of resurrection.

**4. Test of faith versus test of obedience.** Some interpreters emphasize the trial as testing Abraham's *trust in God's promise* (that Isaac must live for the promise to be fulfilled, so Abraham must trust God to keep the promise even through the command to kill), following the logic of Hebrews 11:19. Others, following the plain sense of Genesis 22:12 ("now I know that you fear God"), emphasize simple *obedience* as the virtue being proved — Abraham's willingness to submit even without understanding how the promise could be kept. Sarna, *Genesis* (JPS Torah Commentary, 1989), and Walter Brueggemann, *Genesis* (Interpretation series, 1982), both treat the test primarily as one of unconditional trust and "fear of God" rather than blind obedience for its own sake.

**5. Moral and theological difficulty.** Some modern interpreters, both Jewish and Christian, wrestle openly with the ethical strangeness of a God who commands what elsewhere is condemned. Emmanuel Levinas and other 20th-century Jewish philosophers have engaged the Akedah as raising the problem of whether ethics can ever be suspended by revelation; this is a live philosophical conversation rather than a settled consensus position, and is treated as such by Levenson (1993) in surveying the range of Jewish responses across history.
