## Direct Answer

Qoheleth (the "Teacher" or "Preacher" of Ecclesiastes) is not offering a poem of comfort about life's balance so much as a meditation on human limitation before God's sovereignty over time. The famous catalog of "a time to" (3:1–8) establishes that every human activity has its appointed season, fixed by God, not by human choice. Then 3:9–15 draws the conclusion: since God has set these times and "made everything beautiful in its time" (3:11) while also placing "eternity" in the human heart, people cannot fully grasp the totality of what God is doing. The appropriate response is not mastery or anxiety but humble enjoyment of the moment God gives, and reverent fear of God who governs all of it (3:14). This is a general view, not a specific citation to one scholar alone — though it is widely represented, as noted below.

## Original-Language Notes

I've chosen *ʿēth* and *ʿōlām*, because the whole argument of the passage turns on these two Hebrew words and the tension between them — one bounded, one boundless.

### עֵת (ʿēth)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Ecclesiastes 3:1 — "a time for everything" |
| Lemma | עֵת |
| Parsing | noun, feminine singular |
| Lexicon refs | BDB 773; HALOT 900 |
| Sense here | an appointed, fixed occasion or season — not time in the abstract, but a specific moment ordained for a specific event |

*ʿēth* occurs roughly 29 times in this short unit and anchors the entire poem. BDB and HALOT both give the sense of a "point in time" or "occasion," often with the nuance of divinely fixed timing (as in Psalm 1:3's tree yielding fruit "in its season"). Qoheleth is not saying humans balance their own schedules well; he is saying every human deed falls under a time already set.

### עֹלָם (ʿōlām)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Ecclesiastes 3:11 — "eternity" (BSB) / "the world" (KJV) |
| Lemma | עֹלָם |
| Parsing | noun, masculine singular |
| Lexicon refs | BDB 761; HALOT 798 |
| Sense here | a sense of duration beyond the immediate — "eternity" or "the timeless," contrasted with humanity's limited grasp of time |

This word is the crux of the verse and a genuine translation divide: KJV renders it "the world," following an older tradition, while BSB, ASV, and most modern renderings favor "eternity" or "a sense of time/permanence." HALOT and BDB both support the temporal sense ("long duration, antiquity, futurity") over a spatial "world" meaning here. The point Qoheleth makes: God has placed in the human heart an awareness of, or longing for, the eternal — yet without the ability to comprehend God's full work "from beginning to end." This is the theological hinge of the passage: creatures who sense eternity but live confined to *ʿēth*.

Want the word behind "beautiful" (yāphâ) in 3:11, or "fear" (yārēʾ) in 3:14? Just ask.

## Context & Background

Ecclesiastes is framed as the reflection of "the Preacher, son of David, king in Jerusalem" (1:1), traditionally associated with Solomon, though many modern commentators, including Tremper Longman III (*The Book of Ecclesiastes*, New International Commentary on the Old Testament, 1998), read "Qoheleth" as a literary persona reflecting on wisdom and life's limits, with the book's Hebrew showing linguistic features some date later than Solomon's era. The poem in 3:1–8 uses fourteen pairs of opposites (born/die, plant/uproot, kill/heal, etc.) — a literary device called merism, in which two extremes represent the whole range between them, a technique well attested in ancient Near Eastern wisdom literature (Choon-Leong Seow, *Ecclesiastes*, Anchor Bible, 1997). The structure moves from the poem of times (3:1–8) to theological reflection (3:9–15), asking what profit toil has given humanity in light of this divinely fixed order — a question Qoheleth raises repeatedly throughout the book (cf. 1:3).

## Interpretive Options

- **Determinism/acceptance reading**: God has fixed all times; human wisdom lies in accepting this order rather than resisting it — a view associated with readings that emphasize Qoheleth's realism about human limitation (Michael Fox, *Ecclesiastes*, JPS Bible Commentary, 2004).
- **Carpe diem / gift reading**: The passage's turn to "eat, drink, and find enjoyment" (3:12–13) is read by Longman and others as Qoheleth commending simple joys as God's gift, precisely because ultimate meaning is inaccessible.
- **Eternity-in-the-heart as tension, not comfort**: Seow and others read 3:11 as describing a genuine ache — humans sense there is more than the fleeting moment, yet cannot lay hold of it, producing the book's characteristic melancholy rather than resolution.
