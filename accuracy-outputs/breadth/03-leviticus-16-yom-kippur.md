## Direct Answer

Leviticus 16 sets out Israel's Day of Atonement (Yom Kippur) ritual: Aaron the high priest makes atonement for himself, his household, and the whole assembly through a sequence of sin offerings, incense, and blood applications in the Most Holy Place — culminating in two goats that together form one atonement rite. One goat is slaughtered as a sin offering, its blood used to cleanse the sanctuary itself; the other, the "scapegoat" (Azazel goat), has the people's sins confessed over it and is sent away into the wilderness, carrying them off. The two goats picture two dimensions of atonement — expiation (sin dealt with by death and blood) and removal (sin carried away and separated from the camp).

## Original-Language Notes

The passage turns on two terms: עֲזָאזֵל (Azazel) — because its meaning drives the entire second-goat rite — and כָּפַר (kaphar, "atone"), the verb that names what the whole chapter is doing.

### עֲזָאזֵל (Azazel)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Leviticus 16:8 — "the scapegoat" |
| Lemma | עֲזָאזֵל |
| Parsing | proper noun / rare term, occurs only in Leviticus 16 (vv. 8, 10, 26) |
| Lexicon refs | HALOT; BDB |
| Sense here | disputed — a place/entity to which the goat is sent, or an abstract description of "removal/dismissal" |

This word is a genuine crux. HALOT and BDB register the difficulty: some read it as a proper name (a wilderness demon or place), others derive it from a root suggesting "entire removal" (hence the traditional English rendering "scapegoat," from Tyndale's "the goat that departs"). The KJV, BSB, and most English versions render it functionally ("scapegoat") rather than transliterating, because the referent itself is uncertain.

### כָּפַר (kaphar)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Leviticus 16:30 — "atonement will be made" |
| Lemma | כָּפַר |
| Parsing | verb, Piel stem |
| Lexicon refs | BDB; HALOT |
| Sense here | to atone, make propitiation/expiation, cover or purge sin |

BDB and HALOT both attest the sense "to cover" or "to purge/atone," used throughout Leviticus for the cleansing effect of blood applied to persons and to sacred objects. The term binds together every act in chapter 16 — the priest's own atonement, the sanctuary's cleansing, and the people's cleansing — under a single covering/purging action.

## Context & Background

Leviticus 16 comes immediately after the deaths of Nadab and Abihu (Leviticus 10), who "offered unauthorized fire before the LORD" and died — a note the chapter opens by recalling (Lev 16:1). Gordon Wenham, *The Book of Leviticus* (New International Commentary on the Old Testament, 1979), observes that this framing underscores the danger of approaching God's presence carelessly; the elaborate procedures of chapter 16 exist precisely so the high priest may enter the Most Holy Place safely, and only once a year.

The ritual involved: Aaron bathing and dressing in plain linen rather than his usual ornate high-priestly garments (16:4); a bull sacrificed for his own sin and his household's (16:6, 11); incense burned to cover the mercy seat so he would not die on seeing it (16:12–13); blood from the bull sprinked on and before the mercy seat (16:14); then the two goats "for the people" (16:7–10).

Jacob Milgrom, *Leviticus 1–16* (Anchor Bible, 1991), argues the blood rites cleanse not the people's conscience directly but the sanctuary itself — accumulated impurity from Israel's sins, in his view, defiles the sacred space, and the Day of Atonement purges the tabernacle so God's presence can continue to dwell there.

The "scapegoat" ritual is unique in the sacrificial system: rather than being slain, the live goat bears confessed sin and is driven into the wilderness — a place outside the ordered, inhabited camp, associated in the text with uncleanness and chaos (see the Mishnah's later elaboration in tractate Yoma, which describes a cliff from which the goat was eventually pushed, a post-biblical development beyond the text of Leviticus itself).

## Interpretive Options

**On what Azazel means** — this is a live scholarly question, not settled fact:
- **Wilderness demon/desert entity**: Some interpreters (following ancient Jewish tradition reflected in 1 Enoch and later rabbinic sources) read Azazel as a name for a wilderness spirit to whom the goat is sent, though the goat is not offered *to* it as a sacrifice — it functions as banishment, not worship. Milgrom, *Leviticus 1–16* (Anchor Bible, 1991), discusses this reading while emphasizing the text carefully avoids any sacrificial language toward Azazel.
- **Abstract "removal/entire removal"**: Others, including the translators behind the traditional "scapegoat" rendering, take the term as describing the goat's function (complete removal of sin) rather than naming a being.

**On what the two goats signify together** — this is a general view, not a specific citation, though it is widely reflected across commentaries: the paired goats depict two complementary aspects of atonement — one goat's blood satisfies the need for expiation before God (life given for life, applied to the sanctuary), while the second goat pictures sin's total removal from the community, sent away where it cannot return. Wenham, *The Book of Leviticus* (NICOT, 1979), and Milgrom both note that no single animal could carry both symbolic loads at once, hence the need for two.

**Christian typological reading**: The New Testament book of Hebrews draws on the Day of Atonement's inner-sanctuary entrance and blood application as a type of Christ's greater and once-for-all entrance into the heavenly sanctuary (Hebrews 9:6–12, 24–26). This is a theological/typological reading found in Hebrews itself and developed further by Christian interpreters; it is not a claim about what Leviticus 16 meant to its original Israelite audience but about how the New Testament writer applies the pattern.
