## Direct Answer

Zechariah 12:10 is one of the most textually and interpretively debated verses in the Old Testament. The Masoretic Text (the standard Hebrew text) reads that God says "they shall look on **me** whom they have pierced" — a first-person pronoun referring to YHWH himself, which creates a striking theological puzzle: how can YHWH be "pierced"? Ancient and modern interpreters have handled this tension differently — some reading it as messianic prophecy fulfilled in Christ's crucifixion (John 19:37 quotes this verse directly), others reading it as poetic identification between YHWH and a slain representative, and text critics debating whether the original reading was "me" (elai) or "him" (elav), a difference of a single Hebrew letter.

## Original-Language Notes

The whole crux of this verse rests on two words: the pronoun אֵלַי ("to me") and the verb דָּקָרוּ ("they pierced") — the first because it creates the grammatical shift that generates the entire text-critical debate, the second because its semantic range shapes what "piercing" actually means.

### אֵלַי (elai)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Zechariah 12:10 — "they will look on Me" |
| Lemma | אֶל (preposition) + 1st person singular suffix |
| Parsing | Preposition with pronominal suffix, "to/on me" |
| Lexicon refs | BDB אֶל; HALOT אֶל |
| Sense here | Directional/object marker: "toward me" |
| Range of meaning | The preposition אֶל normally means "to, toward, unto"; with a first-person suffix it yields "to me" or "on me," marking the object of the gaze. |
| Semantic domain | Spatial/directional relation |
| Etymology | Etymology of a preposition offers little semantic payload here; the debate is textual, not etymological — the issue is which pronoun was originally written, not what the root "means." (Avoiding etymological fallacy: the meaning is settled by grammar and manuscript evidence, not word-history.) |

This is the single word the entire controversy hinges on. The Masoretic Text reads אֵלַי ("to me" — first person, i.e., YHWH speaking of himself as pierced). A number of Hebrew manuscripts, however, read אֵלָיו (elav, "to him" — third person). The consonantal difference is minimal (a final vav vs. a yod), which is why this is treated as a classic case of a "difficult reading" that could have been either the original text simplified by scribes, or a smoothing of an original third-person text into first person, or vice versa. Bruce Waltke and Michael Fishbane both note this as one of the traditional Masoretic "tiqqune sopherim" (scribal corrections) discussions, though scholars differ on whether this specific verse belongs in that category.

### דָּקָרוּ (daqaru)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Zechariah 12:10 — "whom they have pierced" |
| Lemma | דָּקַר (daqar) |
| Parsing | Qal perfect, 3rd person plural |
| Lexicon refs | BDB דָּקַר; HALOT דָּקַר |
| Sense here | To pierce through, thrust through, typically with a weapon, often resulting in death |
| Range of meaning | דָּקַר is used elsewhere for fatal piercing by sword or spear (e.g., Numbers 25:8; Judges 9:54; 1 Samuel 31:4) — it consistently denotes violent, lethal wounding, not a metaphorical or emotional "piercing." |
| Semantic domain | Violence / warfare / killing |
| Etymology | The root's usage across the Hebrew Bible in contexts of literal, physical wounding is what matters here — not any speculative root sense — since etymology alone cannot settle whether the piercing in view is historical, metaphorical, or eschatological. |

The verb's consistently violent, physical sense is why the verse creates such theological tension when paired with "me" (YHWH) as object — it implies YHWH being violently, physically wounded, which is precisely why some ancient versions and interpreters modified or reinterpreted the pronoun.

## Context & Background

**Literary and historical setting.** Zechariah 9–14 forms a distinct section often called "Second Zechariah," widely treated by scholars as stylistically and thematically different from Zechariah 1–8, though still part of the canonical book. Paul Hanson (*The Dawn of Apocalyptic*, 1975) and others have discussed this section's apocalyptic, eschatological orientation — looking toward a decisive future day of the Lord, cosmic upheaval, and the restoration of Jerusalem. Zechariah 12 sits within a series of oracles ("the burden of the word of the LORD," 12:1) concerning Jerusalem's future siege, deliverance, and inward transformation.

**Political and religious setting.** The prophet writes into the post-exilic community (Zechariah's ministry is dated to the late sixth century BCE, contemporaneous with Haggai, during the rebuilding of the Second Temple under Persian rule), but chapters 9–14 project forward to an unspecified future "day" of divine judgment and restoration. The mention of "the house of David" (12:10) evokes the ongoing hope for Davidic restoration current in post-exilic prophetic and messianic expectation.

**Geography.** Hadad-rimmon in the plain of Megiddo (12:11) is generally located in the Jezreel Valley, a major agricultural plain in northern Israel — the same region where King Josiah was killed in battle (2 Kings 23:29; 2 Chronicles 35:22–25), an event that became a byword for national mourning. Many commentators, including Carol and Eric Meyers (*Zechariah 9–14*, Anchor Bible, 1993), connect the "mourning of Hadad-rimmon" allusion to that memory of Josiah's death.

**Comparative ANE context.** Mourning rites "as for an only child" and "firstborn" reflect known ancient Near Eastern mourning customs — intense, public lamentation involving wailing, fasting, and ritual grief, attested across the ancient Near East for the loss of an only heir, since an only son's death meant the end of the family line. This cultural background sharpens the emotional weight of the image in 12:10.

**Textual transmission.** The Masoretic Text's אֵלַי is the reading found in the standard Hebrew textual tradition. Some Hebrew manuscripts and some ancient versions reflect the third-person אֵלָיו. The Septuagint (Greek translation, likely third–second century BCE) renders the phrase with a form understood by some as "toward me," broadly supporting the first-person reading, though the Greek syntax of the LXX at this point is itself debated among text critics. Emanuel Tov (*Textual Criticism of the Hebrew Bible*, 2012) discusses the broader phenomenon of pronoun variants between first and third person in the Hebrew textual tradition as one recognized category of scribal variation, without asserting a single resolution for every instance.

## Interpretive Options

**1. Messianic/Christological reading (traditional Christian reading).** The verse is read as a prophecy of the piercing of the Messiah, understood by New Testament writers as fulfilled in Christ's crucifixion. John 19:37 quotes Zechariah 12:10 directly ("they shall look on him whom they pierced") in connection with the piercing of Jesus's side. This reading takes the identification of the "pierced one" with YHWH as reflecting a mysterious unity between YHWH and the Messiah — the pierced one is simultaneously spoken of as YHWH's own self and as a distinguishable "him" mourned by the people. This is the majority reading in the Christian exegetical tradition from the Church Fathers onward; Justin Martyr (2nd century) and later patristic writers cite it messianically.

**2. Corporate/representative reading (some Jewish and historical-critical interpretation).** Some interpreters read the "pierced one" as a representative figure — a slain leader or martyred prophet whose death the community comes to mourn and repent over — rather than a strict individual messianic prediction. Rashi (11th century) and other medieval Jewish commentators associated the verse with the deaths of righteous leaders such as Messiah ben Joseph (a figure in later rabbinic messianic speculation) or historical calamities, rather than with a single crucifixion event.

**3. Text-critical solution via emendation.** Some scholars, troubled by the theological difficulty of YHWH being "pierced," have proposed that אֵלָיו ("to him," third person) was the original reading, later altered to first person, or vice versa — that scribes softened an original "him" (avoiding the shock of divine piercing) is one direction proposed, while others argue the reverse: that "me" is original and "him" a later smoothing to avoid theological difficulty. Carol and Eric Meyers (*Zechariah 9–14*, Anchor Bible, 1993) discuss the manuscript evidence and lean toward treating the first-person reading as original, noting the abrupt shift to third person ("mourn for him") in the same verse as a known feature of Hebrew style (sudden pronoun shifts, sometimes called "enallage"), not necessarily evidence of textual corruption.

**4. Divine identification without emendation.** A number of interpreters (both older Jewish and Christian) take the MT as it stands and read the "me/him" shift not as a textual problem but as intentional prophetic style — YHWH speaks in the first person of the piercing as directed at himself, then shifts to third person as the narrative moves to the people's mourning response. This is sometimes defended as a feature of Hebrew prophetic rhetoric where speaker perspective shifts within a single oracle (compare similar shifts elsewhere in the prophets).
