## Direct Answer

The Shema (Deuteronomy 6:4–9) is Israel's foundational declaration of God's oneness and the command to love him wholly. It has functioned in Judaism as the central confession of monotheistic faith, recited daily in liturgy and bound literally to body and doorpost. In Christian practice, Jesus cites it as "the first and greatest commandment" (Mark 12:29–30), and it undergirds Christian affirmations of God's unity while being read through a trinitarian lens by later theologians.

## Original-Language Notes

Two words carry the weight here: אֶחָד (echad), because the whole theological force of the passage turns on what "one" means, and לֵבָב (levav), because the Hebrew concept of "heart" reaches further than the English word suggests.

### אֶחָד (echad)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Deuteronomy 6:4 — "the LORD is one" |
| Lemma | אֶחָד |
| Parsing | numeral/adjective, masculine singular |
| Lexicon refs | BDB; HALOT |
| Sense here | numerical/unitary oneness, uniqueness |

Echad denotes "one" in a unifying or compound sense (as in Genesis 2:24, "one flesh") as well as simple numerical unity, and BDB and HALOT both attest this range. Jewish interpreters generally read it here as affirming YHWH's singular, exclusive identity against the polytheism of surrounding nations — a claim about uniqueness and indivisibility rather than an explicit statement about inner plurality within God.

### לֵבָב (levav)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Deuteronomy 6:5 — "with all your heart" |
| Lemma | לֵבָב |
| Parsing | noun, masculine singular |
| Lexicon refs | BDB; HALOT |
| Sense here | the whole inner person — mind, will, and emotion |

In Hebrew anthropology, levav is not merely the seat of emotion (as "heart" often implies in English) but includes intellect, will, and moral choice — HALOT and BDB both note this broader semantic range. Loving God with levav means engaging the whole inner life, not just affection.

## Context & Background

Deuteronomy presents itself as Moses's address to Israel on the plains of Moab before entering the land (Deuteronomy 1:1–5). The Shema follows the Ten Commandments (Deuteronomy 5) and serves as Israel's foundational confession amid a Canaanite religious landscape marked by polytheism and idol worship — a setting described by Peter Craigie, *The Book of Deuteronomy* (New International Commentary on the Old Testament, 1976).

The instructions to bind the words "on your hands" and "between your eyes" and to write them on doorposts became the historical basis for tefillin (phylacteries) and mezuzot in later Jewish practice, as documented in the Mishnah tractate Berakhot, which prescribes the Shema's recitation morning and evening. Jeffrey Tigay, *Deuteronomy* (JPS Torah Commentary, 1996), traces how this passage became the daily confession of faith in rabbinic Judaism, recited alongside Deuteronomy 6:4–9, 11:13–21, and Numbers 15:37–41.

## Interpretive Options

- **Jewish liturgical tradition**: The Shema is recited twice daily (morning and evening) as Israel's core confession of God's unity and covenant loyalty, per the Mishnah (Berakhot 1:1–3) — this is a documentable historical practice, not a contested interpretation.
- **Trinitarian Christian reading**: Some Christian theologians read echad as compatible with, though not proving, later trinitarian doctrine — a complex unity rather than a bare numerical singularity. This is a possible interpretation read back into the text; it is not what the Hebrew word asserts on its own terms, as Tigay and other Jewish commentators note.
- **Jesus's use in the Gospels**: Mark 12:29–30 has Jesus quote the Shema as the greatest commandment, joining it to Leviticus 19:18 (love of neighbor) — a pairing discussed by R.T. France, *The Gospel of Mark* (New International Greek Testament Commentary, 2002).
