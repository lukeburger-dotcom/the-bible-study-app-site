## Direct Answer

1 Samuel 28 recounts Saul, desperate on the eve of his final battle, seeking out a medium at En-dor after God has stopped answering him through every legitimate channel. The text says a figure identified as Samuel appears, delivers a real prophecy (Saul's death and Israel's defeat the next day), and that prophecy comes true exactly. Whether this was actually Samuel — or a demonic impersonation, or something else — is a genuine, long-standing dispute among faithful interpreters. The narrative itself is remarkably restrained: it does not editorialize about the metaphysics of what happened, which is precisely why the question has generated centuries of debate. What the text is unambiguous about is the moral verdict: Saul's act of consulting a medium is condemned (1 Chronicles 10:13–14 makes this explicit), regardless of which view one takes of who or what appeared.

## Original-Language Notes

I've chosen two terms — the "medium" description (בַּעֲלַת־אוֹב, ba'alat-'ov) and the word for what she saw ascending (אֱלֹהִים, elohim) — because these are exactly where the English can flatten what's culturally and interpretively loaded in the Hebrew.

### אוֹב ('ov)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | 1 Samuel 28:7 — "a woman that hath a familiar spirit" (KJV); BSB renders this "a medium" |
| Lemma | אוֹב ('ov) |
| Parsing | Noun, masculine singular (here in construct: בַּעֲלַת־אוֹב, "possessor/mistress of an 'ov") |
| Lexicon refs | BDB 15; HALOT 1.20 |
| Sense here | A spirit associated with necromancy, or the medium/practitioner who channels such a spirit; possibly related to a "pit" or ancestral spirit-conduit |
| Range of meaning | Ranges from the spirit itself, to the practice of consulting the dead, to the practitioner (a "ba'alat-ov," literally "possessor of an ov") |
| Semantic domain | Divination / necromancy / forbidden cultic practice |
| Etymology | Possibly related to a root meaning "pit" or connected to ancestor/ghost invocation in other Semitic contexts, though the exact derivation is debated; etymology alone should not be used to settle the term's functional meaning in this narrative (etymological fallacy caution — usage in context, not word origin, determines sense). |

The term appears throughout the Torah's prohibitions (Leviticus 19:31; 20:6, 27; Deuteronomy 18:11) as one of several forbidden divinatory practices, which is the legal backdrop Saul is violating — he had himself outlawed such practitioners (1 Samuel 28:3, 9).

### אֱלֹהִים (elohim)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | 1 Samuel 28:13 — "I saw gods ascending out of the earth" (KJV); BSB reads "I see a spirit form ascending from the ground" |
| Lemma | אֱלֹהִים (elohim) |
| Parsing | Noun, masculine plural in form (common as a plural-of-majesty, frequently singular in sense) |
| Lexicon refs | BDB 43; HALOT 1.52 |
| Sense here | Here likely "a supernatural/spirit being" rather than "God" or "gods" in the polytheistic sense — the term has a wide semantic range covering God, gods, angels, and other numinous beings |
| Range of meaning | Spans "God" (the God of Israel), "gods" (pagan deities), "divine/supernatural beings" generally, and occasionally "judges" or "mighty ones" in a few contested texts |
| Semantic domain | Divine/supernatural beings |
| Etymology | Plural form of a root related to "god/might"; the plural morphology should not be read as implying polytheism here — this is a known point of caution against etymological or morphological over-reading. |

This word is the crux of the ambiguity: the medium doesn't say "I see Samuel," she says "I see an elohim/spirit-being" ascending, and only after Saul's follow-up question does the figure get identified as Samuel by his appearance ("an old man... covered with a mantle"). The narrator's own voice, however, does say plainly "Samuel said to Saul" (28:15–16), which is a major piece of evidence in the debate below.

## Context & Background

**Historical/narrative setting.** This scene falls immediately before Saul's death (1 Samuel 31) and is deliberately placed by the narrator between two panels of David's story (1 Samuel 27 and 29), a structuring device many commentators note — Robert Alter, *The David Story* (1999), discusses how this positioning heightens the tragic irony of Saul's final night. The Philistines have mustered at Shunem; Saul is camped at Gilboa (1 Samuel 28:4), and En-dor lay across the Jezreel valley, behind or near Philistine lines — meaning Saul's night journey was also physically dangerous, undertaken in disguise (28:8).

**Cultural/legal background.** Saul had earlier "expelled the mediums and spiritists from the land" (1 Samuel 28:3, BSB), enforcing the Torah's prohibitions on necromancy (Leviticus 19:31; 20:6, 27; Deuteronomy 18:10–11). His visit to the medium is thus a direct reversal of his own policy and of covenant law — a point the text is at pains to make explicit through his disguise and the woman's fearful reaction.

**ANE comparative context.** Necromancy (consultation of the dead for guidance) was practiced across the ancient Near East — attested in Mesopotamian and Ugaritic sources involving rituals to summon ancestral spirits for oracles, as surveyed in general treatments such as the *Anchor Bible Dictionary* entries on divination and necromancy. Israel's law set itself apart from neighboring nations precisely by banning these practices and directing inquiry to Yahweh through prophets, priests (Urim), and dreams instead (cf. 1 Samuel 28:6, which lists exactly these three legitimate channels Saul had already tried and found silent).

**Geography.** En-dor sat on the northern side of the Jezreel valley, near Mount Tabor, some distance from Saul's camp at Gilboa — commentators such as Ralph Klein, *1 Samuel* (Word Biblical Commentary, 1983), note the geographic tension this creates for Saul's covert night crossing.

## Interpretive Options

This is one of the most debated narrative-theological questions in the Old Testament. Major positions, with representative names:

**1. It really was Samuel, by God's sovereign act.** On this view, God overrode the medium's normal practice (she herself is startled and cries out — 28:12 — suggesting this was *not* her usual result) and sovereignly permitted Samuel to appear and deliver a true, divinely authorized prophecy. Proponents include many older commentators and some modern ones — e.g., this reading is defended in various forms by scholars who stress that the narrator flatly states "Samuel said" (28:15–16) without qualification, and that the prophecy given is entirely accurate (fulfilled in chapter 31), which fits God's character more than a demon's. Ralph Klein, *1 Samuel* (Word Biblical Commentary, 1983), surveys this position among others.

**2. It was a demonic deception impersonating Samuel.** This view, held historically by figures such as Tertullian (c. 155–c. 220) in his treatise *On the Soul*, and later reflected in some Reformation-era commentary, holds that God does not grant necromancers access to the righteous dead, and that Scripture elsewhere (e.g., Luke 16:19–31, the rich man and Lazarus; 2 Corinthians 11:14, Satan disguised as an angel of light) indicates evil spirits can impersonate the dead to deceive. On this reading, the medium's spirit-familiar produced an apparition that spoke truths permitted by God for judgment on Saul, but was not literally Samuel's soul.

**3. The medium was a fraud, and the narrative depicts her trickery (possibly with Saul's own guilt/expectation filling in the rest).** Some interpreters, particularly influenced by a more skeptical or rationalist historical-critical reading, suggest the entire scene is staged deception — the medium, perhaps with confederates, stages an illusion, and Saul, already terrified, projects Samuel onto it. This view struggles against the narrator's own flat statement "Samuel said to Saul" and the fulfilled prophecy, and is a minority position even among critical scholars; it's noted as one option surveyed in P. Kyle McCarter, *I Samuel* (Anchor Bible, 1980), though McCarter treats the narrative's own claims about Samuel's appearance seriously as the text's intent.

**4. Soul sleep / conditional immortality readings.** Some traditions (e.g., certain strands of Adventist or Christadelphian theology) that hold the dead are unconscious until resurrection read this passage as evidence *against* a literal appearance of Samuel's spirit, favoring option 2 or 3 on theological grounds independent of the narrative details.

Commentators broadly agree, whichever view is taken, that the text's own moral judgment is unambiguous: 1 Chronicles 10:13–14 states Saul died "for his unfaithfulness... and also for asking counsel of a medium, to seek guidance" (BSB), naming this consultation as a contributing cause of his downfall, alongside his disobedience to the Lord's word. The narrative does not commend the practice regardless of who or what appeared.
