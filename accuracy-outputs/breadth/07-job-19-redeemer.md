## Direct Answer
Job declares his confidence that a living "Redeemer" (Hebrew *go'el*) will one day stand upon the earth and vindicate him — even after his body has been destroyed. Whether this is a clear statement of bodily resurrection or a hope for post-mortem vindication before God is genuinely debated among commentators; the Hebrew of verses 26–27 is notoriously difficult, and translations diverge sharply at exactly the point where the "resurrection" reading would need to be secured.

## Original-Language Notes

The word that carries the whole crux is *go'el*, with the disputed phrase "in/without my flesh" running a close second — the rest of the English is stable enough that the argument lives almost entirely in these two spots.

### גֹּאֲלִי (go'ali)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Job 19:25 — "my Redeemer" |
| Lemma | גָּאַל (ga'al) |
| Parsing | Qal participle with 1st person singular suffix, functioning as noun |
| Lexicon refs | BDB; HALOT |
| Sense here | one who acts as kinsman-redeemer — buying back property, avenging blood, or rescuing a relative from distress |

In Israelite law the *go'el* is a near kinsman with legal obligation to redeem (Leviticus 25:25; Ruth 4), or an avenger of blood (Numbers 35:19). Job applies the term to God, or possibly a divine advocate distinct from God, expecting personal vindication. David J.A. Clines, *Job 21–37* (Word Biblical Commentary, 2006), notes the term's legal-family background is essential — Job is claiming someone with the right and duty to plead his case.

### מִבְּשָׂרִי (mibbesari)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Job 19:26 — "in my flesh" |
| Lemma | בָּשָׂר (basar) with preposition מִן (min) |
| Parsing | Noun, masculine singular, with prefixed preposition and 1st singular suffix |
| Lexicon refs | BDB; HALOT |
| Sense here | ambiguous — the preposition מִן can mean "from within" (i.e., in my flesh) or "away from / apart from" (i.e., without my flesh) |

This is the pivot of the whole controversy. If מִן means "from within my flesh," Job expects to see God while still (or again) embodied. If it means "apart from my flesh" — after the body is gone — the verse describes a disembodied vision, not bodily resurrection. Francis I. Andersen, *Job* (Tyndale Old Testament Commentaries, 1976), and John E. Hartley, *The Book of Job* (New International Commentary on the Old Testament, 1988), both flag this ambiguity as unresolved by grammar alone.

Want the word behind "stand" (v. 25) or "another" (v. 27)? Just ask.

## Context & Background

Job speaks these words in the middle of his third response to his friends, after Bildad's second speech has painted the fate of the wicked in grim terms (Job 18). David J.A. Clines (WBC, 2006) situates 19:25–27 as the emotional peak of Job's speech — having just described his total abandonment by family, friends, and even servants (19:13–20), Job pivots from despair to a sudden, almost defiant assertion of hope.

The historical-legal background of *go'el* matters here: in ancient Israelite society, a kinsman-redeemer had the duty to redeem land, marry a widowed relative, or avenge wrongful death when the person harmed could not act for himself (Leviticus 25; Ruth 3–4; Numbers 35). Job, stripped of every human advocate, reaches for the image of a kinsman who will one day stand up and plead his cause — even, Job says, after Job himself is dead.

John E. Hartley (NICOT, 1988) notes that Job has already spoken earlier in the book of an "arbiter" or "witness in heaven" (Job 9:33; 16:19) who might mediate between him and God. Many commentators read 19:25–27 as the climactic development of that theme — not a doctrine of bodily resurrection dropped fully formed into the text, but Job's growing hope that someone will vindicate him against the accusation that his suffering proves guilt.

## Interpretive Options

**1. Bodily resurrection.** Some older and confessional readings (reflected in much of the KJV-era Christian tradition, and articulated by writers such as Matthew Henry in his commentary) take this as Job's anticipation of bodily resurrection and final vision of God in the flesh, later fulfilled in Christ. This reading leans on "in my flesh shall I see God" (KJV) taken straightforwardly.

**2. Vindication in this life or at death, not bodily resurrection.** Many modern critical commentators — including David J.A. Clines (WBC, 2006) and Norman Habel, *The Book of Job* (Old Testament Library, 1985) — argue Job expects a legal vindication, possibly even a vision of God at the moment of death or shortly after, but not a doctrine of bodily resurrection as later articulated in Daniel 12:2 or the New Testament. They point to the ambiguity of "from/apart from my flesh" and note Job elsewhere insists death is final and irreversible (Job 7:9–10; 14:10–12).

**3. A hoped-for encounter after death, embodied or not, left genuinely open.** Francis Andersen (Tyndale, 1976) and John Hartley (NICOT, 1988) both resist forcing a firm resurrection doctrine onto the Hebrew, while still recognizing this as an extraordinary flash of hope that God will ultimately vindicate Job face to face — whatever the exact mode.

This is a genuinely contested question at the grammatical level, not simply a modern-versus-traditional divide — the Hebrew text itself is the site of the disagreement.
