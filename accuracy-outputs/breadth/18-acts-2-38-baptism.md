## Direct Answer

Acts 2:38 is one of the most theologically contested single verses in the New Testament because of the Greek preposition translated "for" (εἰς, *eis*) connecting baptism to "the forgiveness of sins." The verse can be read as teaching that baptism is instrumental in receiving forgiveness, or that baptism is the outward response *because of* / *on the basis of* forgiveness already granted through repentance and faith. Major Christian traditions land differently here, and the grammatical evidence itself is genuinely ambiguous — this is not a case where the Greek settles the question outright. I'll lay out the text, the language crux, and the major interpretive traditions with their representative names.

## Original-Language Notes

I chose **εἰς** (eis) and **μετανοήσατε** (metanoēsate) — the first because the whole debate turns on this one preposition, the second because "repent" carries a fuller sense than the English word suggests and shapes how the clause should be read as a unit.

### εἰς (eis)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Acts 2:38 — "for the forgiveness of your sins" |
| Lemma | εἰς |
| Parsing | preposition + accusative (ἄφεσιν, accusative singular) |
| Lexicon refs | BDAG; Louw-Nida 90 (domain of relations, marking goal/purpose or cause/basis) |
| Range of meaning | "into, unto, for, with a view to" (purpose/result); also attested as "on account of, because of" (causal, backward-looking) in some contexts |
| Semantic domain | Spatial/relational preposition marking goal, purpose, result, reference, or (debated) cause |
| Etymology | Eis derives from a common Greek directional preposition; etymology alone cannot settle its function here — usage in context governs meaning, and both purpose and causal uses are attested elsewhere in Koine Greek (fallacy of etymology caution). |
| Sense here | Disputed — either "resulting in forgiveness" (telic/purpose) or "because of / on the basis of forgiveness already given" (causal) |

This is the crux of the whole verse. BDAG lists both goal-directed and, in some disputed instances, causal-retrospective uses of *eis*, and grammarians disagree on which fits Acts 2:38. Some point to Matthew 12:41 ("repented at [eis] the preaching of Jonah") as a possible causal parallel, though this parallel is itself debated among grammarians.

### μετανοήσατε (metanoēsate)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Acts 2:38 — "Repent" |
| Lemma | μετανοέω |
| Parsing | verb, aorist active imperative, 2nd person plural |
| Lexicon refs | BDAG; Louw-Nida 41.52 |
| Range of meaning | "to change one's mind/purpose," in NT usage regularly meaning a turning from sin toward God — moral and spiritual reorientation, not mere intellectual reconsideration |
| Semantic domain | Domain of moral/religious change of behavior and thought |
| Etymology | Meta ("after/change") + noeō ("to think") gives a literal sense of "think afterward" or "change one's mind" — but by NT usage the word has developed a fuller theological sense of repentance/conversion, and should not be reduced to its component parts. |
| Sense here | A summons to turn from sin and toward God in response to the gospel Peter has just preached (Acts 2:22–36) |

Repentance is grammatically the primary imperative of the sentence; "be baptized" (βαπτισθήτω, third person singular passive imperative) is subordinate to it in some grammatical analyses, which some interpreters use to argue baptism's dependent, symbolic role — though others dispute how much weight the grammar alone can bear.

## Context & Background

**Historical setting.** This is Peter's sermon at Pentecost, delivered in Jerusalem to a Jewish and God-fearing audience gathered for the feast (Acts 2:1, 2:5). The audience has just heard Peter's argument from Joel and the Psalms that Jesus, whom they crucified, is Lord and Christ (Acts 2:14–36). Craig Keener, *Acts: An Exegetical Commentary* (2012), situates this sermon within Second Temple Jewish expectation of eschatological restoration and outpoured Spirit, tied to Joel 2:28–32, which Peter has just quoted (Acts 2:17–21).

**Baptism in Second Temple Judaism.** Jewish proselyte baptism and the baptism of John the Baptist (Mark 1:4, "a baptism of repentance for the forgiveness of sins") provide the immediate background. Joachim Jeremias and other scholars of early Judaism note that ritual washing for purification and initiation was a known category in Jewish practice (e.g., at Qumran, per the Dead Sea Scrolls), which would make Peter's hearers unlikely to separate the physical act from its spiritual meaning the way later Western theology often does. This is a general view drawn from comparative Second Temple Judaism scholarship, not a specific citation.

**Geography.** Jerusalem in the first century had numerous *mikvaot* (ritual immersion pools), archaeologically attested near the Temple Mount steps, used for ritual purification before temple worship — evidence, per archaeologist Ronny Reich's excavation reports on Jerusalem's Second Temple period pools, that immersion imagery was deeply embedded in the daily religious life of Peter's audience.

**Rhetorical structure.** The sermon culminates a pattern seen elsewhere in Acts (e.g., Acts 3:19; Acts 22:16) linking repentance, baptism, and forgiveness in various combinations, which interpreters use to argue for their preferred reading of how tightly these three elements bind together.

## Interpretive Options

**1. Baptismal/sacramental view (baptism as instrumental in forgiveness).** Roman Catholic, Eastern Orthodox, Lutheran, and many in the Restoration Movement (Churches of Christ, Disciples of Christ) traditions read *eis* as telic/purposive: baptism is the means by which forgiveness and the Spirit are conveyed, tied to repentant faith. The Catholic Catechism (drawing on Augustine's sacramental theology, *c.* 354–430) and Lutheran confessional documents (the Augsburg Confession, 1530) both affirm baptism's efficacy in conferring grace, though they differ on infant baptism and the mechanism. Alexander Campbell and the Stone-Campbell tradition read Acts 2:38 as establishing baptism as necessary to the reception of forgiveness in this dispensation.

**2. Baptism as the outward sign/seal of a forgiveness already granted through faith (causal/symbolic view).** Many in the Reformed and Baptist traditions read *eis* causally — "because of" forgiveness already secured through repentance and faith — with baptism functioning as the public, symbolic testimony to an inward reality. This is the position argued by A.T. Robertson, *Word Pictures in the New Testament* (1930), who noted the grammatical difficulty but leaned toward reading baptism as testimony rather than cause. John A. Broadus and, more recently, Baptist theologians such as Wayne Grudem, *Systematic Theology* (1994), hold this view, appealing to the wider NT pattern (e.g., Ephesians 2:8–9; Romans 4) where forgiveness is tied to faith apart from ritual acts.

**3. Baptism as integrally united with repentance and faith without resolving instrumentality (a "both/and," non-mechanistic view).** Scholars such as F.F. Bruce, *The Book of Acts* (New International Commentary on the New Testament, 1988), and I. Howard Marshall, *Acts* (Tyndale New Testament Commentaries, 1980), argue that in the apostolic church, repentance, faith, and baptism were not conceptually separated the way later systematic theology separates them — baptism was simply the assumed, immediate response of a believing convert, and the passage does not intend to settle later debates about baptismal regeneration versus symbolic ordinance at all. Marshall notes the danger of importing later doctrinal categories onto a text written before those categories existed as live options.

**4. Grammatical minority view — eis as strictly causal, paralleling Matthew 12:41 and Matthew 3:11.** Some grammarians (cited variously in the debate, e.g., J.R. Mantey in mid-20th-century journal exchanges with Ralph Marcus) argued for a strong causal reading of *eis* here, though this reading has been challenged by other grammarians on the grounds that Mantey's proposed lexical parallels for causal *eis* are contested. This remains a minority position within Greek grammatical scholarship on the point, and this is a general characterization rather than a documented consensus survey.

**Common ground.** Despite the divide, all major traditions agree the verse does *not* teach that baptism is a human work earning merit apart from grace; the disagreement is over baptism's *function* — instrumental means, sign/seal, or integrated response — not over whether grace and Christ's work are the ultimate ground of forgiveness.
