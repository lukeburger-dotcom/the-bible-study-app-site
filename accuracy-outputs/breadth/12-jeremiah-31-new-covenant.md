## Direct Answer

Jeremiah 31:31–34 promises a "new covenant" with Israel and Judah — distinct from the Sinai covenant, which Israel broke. Its core features: God's law written on hearts rather than tablets, an intimate covenant formula ("I will be their God, and they shall be My people"), universal knowledge of God among covenant members, and definitive forgiveness of sin. Hebrews 8 quotes this passage in full to argue that Jesus mediates this very covenant, and Hebrews 10 returns to it to ground the finality of Christ's sacrifice. In Hebrews' argument, the new covenant isn't merely predicted but inaugurated and functioning through Christ's priesthood and death.

## Original-Language Notes

Two terms carry the weight here: *berith chadashah* ("new covenant"), because the entire promise hinges on what "new" means, and *chesed*-adjacent *ba'al* ("husband"), because it frames the old covenant as a broken marriage, not just a broken contract.

### חֲדָשָׁה (chadashah)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Jeremiah 31:31 — "new covenant" |
| Lemma | חָדָשׁ (chadash) |
| Parsing | adjective, feminine singular, modifying berith |
| Lexicon refs | HALOT; BDB |
| Sense here | new in kind/quality, not merely new in time |

HALOT and BDB both attest *chadash* as "new, fresh" — the term doesn't by itself specify whether this is a renewal of the old arrangement or something categorically different. That question is resolved by the passage's content (v. 32: "not like the covenant I made with their fathers") more than by the adjective alone. Hebrews leans hard on this contrast (Hebrews 8:13) to argue the first covenant is "obsolete."

### בַּעַלְתִּי (ba'alti)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Jeremiah 31:32 — "though I was a husband to them" |
| Lemma | בָּעַל (ba'al) |
| Parsing | verb, Qal perfect, 1st common singular, with 3rd masculine plural suffix |
| Lexicon refs | BDB; HALOT |
| Sense here | to be master/husband to; here, marital headship |

BDB and HALOT both list the marital sense of *ba'al* alongside "to own/rule." The verse casts the Sinai covenant in marriage terms — Israel's breach isn't just legal violation but covenantal infidelity, which colors the "new covenant" as a restored and unbreakable marriage bond rather than a mere administrative update.

## Context & Background

Jeremiah prophesied in the final decades of Judah's kingdom, through the Babylonian conquest and exile (late 7th–early 6th century BC), as summarized by J.A. Thompson, *The Book of Jeremiah* (New International Commentary on the Old Testament, 1980). Chapters 30–33 form the "Book of Consolation," a rare block of hope embedded in a book otherwise dominated by judgment oracles — addressed to a nation about to lose land, temple, and monarchy. The old covenant in view is the Sinai/Mosaic covenant (Exodus 19–24), which Jeremiah elsewhere repeatedly indicts Israel for breaking (e.g., Jeremiah 11:1–13). Against that backdrop, the promise of a covenant Israel *cannot* break — because God changes the location of the law from tablets to hearts — functions as the answer to exile's deepest problem: not just displacement, but a heart unable to keep covenant faithfully.

## Interpretive Options

**How Hebrews uses the passage:** Hebrews 8:6–13 quotes Jeremiah 31:31–34 in full as scriptural proof that a new covenant was always anticipated and that Christ is its mediator. Hebrews 8:13 draws the inference: "By calling this covenant 'new,' He has made the first one obsolete." Hebrews 10:15–17 returns to verses 33–34 specifically to argue that Christ's single sacrifice accomplishes the forgiveness the new covenant promises, rendering repeated sacrifice unnecessary. This is documented in the text of Hebrews itself.

**Who are the covenant partners — Israel/Judah specifically, or the church?** Jeremiah's oracle names "the house of Israel and the house of Judah" explicitly (v. 31). Hebrews applies the promise to the church gathered around Christ without re-naming Israel. Covenant theologians (e.g., in the Reformed tradition broadly) read this as the church inheriting/fulfilling the promise as true Israel; dispensational interpreters (e.g., in the tradition following Charles Ryrie) hold that the promise retains a future, literal fulfillment for ethnic Israel, with the church currently participating in the new covenant's benefits without exhausting its referent. This is a live interpretive divide rather than a settled question — named here as such rather than resolved.

**"Inward parts" / heart vs. external law:** A common view (see Thompson, NICOT, 1980) reads this not as abolishing law but as promising internalized obedience — the problem was never the law's content but Israel's capacity to keep it (cf. Jeremiah 17:9). This is a general view, not a single specific citation beyond Thompson.
