## Direct Answer

Isaiah 53 (which actually begins at 52:13) is the fourth and climactic "Servant Song" in Isaiah, describing a figure who suffers, is disfigured, dies, and is "numbered with the transgressors" — yet whose suffering is portrayed as bearing the sin and sickness of others, and who is ultimately vindicated and exalted. The identity of the Servant has been debated since antiquity: Jewish interpretation has read the figure as Israel (corporately), a righteous remnant, or specific individuals (Moses, Hezekiah, Jeremiah, or a future Messiah), while Christian interpretation from the New Testament onward reads it as messianic prophecy fulfilled in Jesus. The New Testament quotes or alludes to this chapter more than any other Old Testament passage in connection with Jesus's suffering, death, and resurrection.

This same word appears throughout Isaiah 40–55 applied explicitly to Israel/Jacob (e.g., Isaiah 41:8; 44:1) and yet in the songs (42:1–4; 49:1–6; 50:4–9; 52:13–53:12) takes on features — a mission *to* Israel, innocent suffering, individual death — that many interpreters find hard to map onto the nation as a whole. This tension is the engine of the identity debate below.

| Range of meaning | נָשָׂא spans literal lifting, bearing responsibility, and forgiving/bearing away sin; סָבַל is more consistently used of bearing heavy loads or suffering |

The pairing of these verbs is significant because נָשָׂא especially is used elsewhere for bearing *iniquity* in a way that results in forgiveness or removal (Leviticus 16:22), which is why many commentators — both Jewish and Christian — see substitutionary or vicarious categories at work here, whatever their view of the Servant's identity.

The translation choice between "pierced" (BSB, following older Jewish and Christian tradition of reading this as violent, fatal injury) and "wounded" (KJV, slightly more general) reflects a real difference in how vividly the text depicts physical suffering — this is part of why Christian readers have long connected this verse to crucifixion imagery, though the Hebrew itself does not specify the *means* of death.

## Context & Background

**Literary setting.** Isaiah 52:13–53:12 is widely recognized as the fourth of four "Servant Songs" in Isaiah 40–55, a category first identified by Bernhard Duhm in his 1892 commentary on Isaiah, though scholars dispute the songs' precise boundaries and whether they should be isolated from their surrounding chapters at all (Christopher R. Seitz, *Isaiah 40–66*, New Interpreter's Bible, 2001, discusses this critical history). The passage falls near the end of what is often called "Second Isaiah" (chapters 40–55), addressed to a community facing or emerging from the Babylonian exile (6th century B.C.).

**Historical setting.** The historical context assumed by Isaiah 40–55 is the Babylonian exile and the prospect of return under Cyrus of Persia (mentioned by name in Isaiah 44:28 and 45:1), whose decree permitting the Jews' return is dated to 538 B.C. The community addressed is one that has experienced national catastrophe — the destruction of Jerusalem and the Temple in 586 B.C. — and questions of suffering, guilt, and restoration are urgent pastoral and theological concerns.

**Genre and form.** The passage is structured as a lament-like report, moving from divine announcement (52:13–15, God speaking of the Servant's exaltation) to a communal confession (53:1–6, "we" who once despised him now confess their misjudgment) to narrative description of his suffering and death (53:7–9) to a final divine pronouncement of vindication (53:10–12). Claus Westermann (*Isaiah 40–66*, Old Testament Library, 1969) analyzes this as adapting the form of a communal confession of guilt combined with elements resembling a royal or prophetic report.

**Comparative ANE background.** Suffering righteous figures and vicarious suffering motifs appear elsewhere in the ancient Near East, though direct literary dependence is not established; John Goldingay (*Isaiah*, New International Biblical Commentary, 2001) cautions against overstating parallels while noting the passage's distinctiveness in linking suffering explicitly to bearing the sins of *others* rather than merely lamenting undeserved suffering.

**Material and cultural notes.** The imagery of "a lamb led to slaughter" (53:7) draws on sacrificial and pastoral imagery familiar in ancient Israelite agrarian life; the reference to the Servant's grave being "with the wicked" and "with a rich man in his death" (53:9) reflects ancient Near Eastern burial customs where the manner and location of burial carried significant honor or shame — this is one of the more textually and interpretively debated verses in the chapter.

## Interpretive Options

**1. The Servant as Israel (corporate reading).** This is the dominant reading in the history of Jewish interpretation, particularly from the medieval period onward — Rashi (1040–1105) and Ibn Ezra (1089–1167) both identify the Servant as the nation of Israel, suffering exile on account of and among the nations, later vindicated. This reading draws support from the explicit identification of "My Servant" as "Israel" and "Jacob" earlier in Isaiah 40–55 (e.g., Isaiah 41:8–9; 44:1; 49:3).

**2. The Servant as a righteous remnant within Israel.** Some scholars, noting that 49:5–6 seems to distinguish the Servant's mission *to* Jacob/Israel from Israel itself, propose the Servant represents a faithful remnant who suffers on behalf of, or representatively for, the wider nation. H. G. M. Williamson (*Variations on a Theme: King, Messiah and Servant in the Book of Isaiah*, 1998) surveys this line of interpretation.

**3. The Servant as a specific historical individual.** Various Jewish interpreters have proposed identifications including Moses, Jeremiah, or Hezekiah, seeing the song as reflecting on the suffering of a particular righteous figure from Israel's past. Origen (c. 184–c. 253), in *Contra Celsum*, reports that some Jewish interpreters in his own day understood the passage of the people collectively, while noting others held individual readings — indicating this diversity is ancient.

**4. The Servant as the coming Messiah (messianic reading).** This is the reading found throughout the New Testament (see below) and in strands of ancient Jewish interpretation as well — the Targum Jonathan (an Aramaic paraphrase of the Prophets, redacted in the early centuries A.D. though preserving earlier tradition) explicitly renders Isaiah 52:13 as referring to "My servant the Messiah," though it notably reassigns much of the suffering language away from the Messiah figure onto Israel's enemies — a move commentators such as Joseph Blenkinsopp (*Isaiah 40–55*, Anchor Bible, 2002) discuss as reflecting theological discomfort with a suffering messianic figure. The overwhelming trajectory of Christian interpretation, from the New Testament forward through the patristic, medieval, and Reformation periods, has read the chapter as directly messianic and fulfilled in Jesus.

**Synthesis note (this is a general summary, not a settled fact):** many contemporary evangelical scholars (e.g., John Oswalt, *The Book of Isaiah, Chapters 40–66*, New International Commentary on the Old Testament, 1998) argue the "Israel" and "individual Messiah" readings need not be exclusive — Isaiah's Servant language may deliberately move between corporate and individual referents, with the individual Servant fulfilling the vocation Israel as a whole failed to embody. This is a possible interpretation drawing the strands together, not a scholarly consensus.

The New Testament's use of Isaiah 53 is extensive: beyond the direct citations above, allusions appear in Mark 15:28 (some manuscripts), Luke 22:37 ("he was numbered with the transgressors," directly quoting Isaiah 53:12), John 12:38 (quoting Isaiah 53:1), Romans 4:25 and 10:16, and Philippians 2:5–11 in its pattern of humiliation-then-exaltation, which N. T. Wright (*The Climax of the Covenant*, 1991) and others argue reflects the shape of Isaiah 52:13–53:12 itself. The volume and placement of these citations — particularly at the heart of the earliest Christian preaching about the cross — is a major reason Isaiah 53 has functioned as the central Old Testament text for Christian atonement theology since the apostolic era.
