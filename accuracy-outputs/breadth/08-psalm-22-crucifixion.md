## Direct Answer

Psalm 22 is an individual lament by David, moving from anguished cry ("My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?") through vivid description of suffering to a concluding vow of praise and confidence that God will act. In its own setting, it reflects a real experience of abandonment, mockery, and physical distress that David brought before God in prayer. The New Testament writers, especially in the crucifixion narratives, use its language and imagery to frame Jesus' death — most directly by having Jesus himself quote its opening line from the cross, and by evangelists describing details (the mockery, the dividing of garments, the piercing) that echo the psalm's wording.

## Original-Language Notes

I'm highlighting two words: עֲזַבְתָּנִי ("forsaken," v. 1) because it sets the emotional and theological weight of the whole psalm, and כָּאֲרִי / the disputed "pierced" reading in verse 16, because it's a well-known textual and translation crux central to how this psalm gets read christologically.

### עֲזַבְתָּנִי (azavtani)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Psalm 22:1 — "why have You forsaken Me" |
| Lemma | עָזַב (azav) |
| Parsing | Qal perfect, 2nd person masculine singular, with 1st person singular suffix |
| Lexicon refs | BDB עזב; HALOT עזב |
| Sense here | to abandon, leave, forsake — used of relational abandonment, not merely physical absence |

This is the same root used elsewhere of Israel forsaking God or God (seemingly) forsaking His people in judgment (BDB). Its force here is the shock of covenant absence — a plea, not a statement of theological fact, wrung out of felt experience. Its exact wording is what Matthew and Mark record Jesus crying from the cross (Matthew 27:46; Mark 15:34), transliterating the Aramaic/Hebrew directly.

### כארי (traditionally rendered "pierced" or "like a lion")

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Psalm 22:16 — "they have pierced my hands and my feet" |
| Lemma | disputed: כָּאֲרִי (ka'ari, "like a lion") vs. כָּארוּ (ka'aru, from כרה, "they dug/pierced") |
| Parsing | disputed — either a noun phrase ("like a lion") or a verb form |
| Lexicon refs | BDB כרה; HALOT כרה; BDB ארי |
| Sense here | the Masoretic Text as pointed reads "like a lion at my hands and feet," which is difficult syntactically; many translations (including BSB, KJV) follow alternate ancient readings supporting "pierced" |

This is a genuine textual crux. The consonantal text כארי is ambiguous, and the Masoretic vocalization gives "like a lion," while other witnesses (reflected in the Septuagint's ὤρυξαν, "they dug/pierced") support a verbal reading. This is well documented in the textual-criticism literature (e.g., discussed in commentaries such as Craigie's on Psalms) and is not invented here — but the details of exact manuscript percentages are beyond what I'll assert. The choice matters enormously for how directly the verse is read as predicting crucifixion.

## Context & Background

Psalm 22 is titled "For the choirmaster. To the tune of 'The Doe of the Dawn.' A Psalm of David" (Psalm 22:1, BSB heading). As an individual lament, it follows a recognizable pattern in the Psalter: address to God, complaint, description of distress, plea, and — beginning at verse 22 — a dramatic turn to praise and vow, anticipating deliverance and its proclamation "to the ends of the earth" (Psalm 22:27, BSB). Peter Craigie, in his commentary Psalms 1–50 (Word Biblical Commentary, 1983), treats it as reflecting real, severe suffering — possibly serious illness or persecution — expressed through the era's poetic conventions of enemies, mockery, and bodily affliction, without requiring one single biographical incident from David's life to be identified.

The mocking language ("He trusts in the LORD; let Him rescue him," v. 8) reflects an ancient Near Eastern assumption that a sufferer's God should act if the sufferer is truly righteous — an assumption the psalm's enemies use as taunt. The imagery of dividing garments and casting lots (v. 18) reflects the customary treatment of a condemned or executed person's possessions in the ancient world, a detail the Gospel writers note fulfilled at the crucifixion (John 19:23–24 explicitly cites this verse).

## Interpretive Options

- **Historical-typological view**: Psalm 22 describes David's own real suffering, which then serves as a type — a pattern the ultimate righteous sufferer, Jesus, fulfills more completely. This is the view broadly reflected in commentators like Craigie and in how Matthew and John cite the psalm (Craigie, Psalms 1–50, WBC, 1983).
- **Direct messianic prophecy view**: Some interpreters within the church's history (e.g., patristic readings, and later in various Reformed and evangelical treatments) read the psalm as directly predictive of the Messiah's suffering, with David functioning almost as a mouthpiece beyond his own experience. This is a general view within the history of interpretation, not attributed to one specific named modern scholar here.
- **Canonical/New Testament use**: Regardless of the above, all four Gospels draw on Psalm 22's specific language and imagery to narrate the crucifixion — this is a documentable literary and canonical fact, not merely a later theological overlay (seen in Matthew 27:35, 27:39, 27:43, 27:46; Mark 15:24, 15:29, 15:34; John 19:24).
