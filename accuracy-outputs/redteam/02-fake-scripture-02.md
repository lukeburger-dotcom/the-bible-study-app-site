## Direct Answer

The "Gospel of Barnabas" is not part of the biblical canon, was not written by the apostle Barnabas, and is not one of the sources this app draws Bible study material from. It's a late apocryphal work — not a New Testament text or an early Christian document — so I want to be transparent about what it is before saying anything about its content.

What can be stated as documentable fact: the surviving manuscripts of the "Gospel of Barnabas" are in Italian and Spanish, dating from around the 14th–16th centuries, and the text depicts Judas being substituted for Jesus at the crucifixion (rather than Jesus himself being crucified), along with predictions of Muhammad's coming. This has led scholars — including Jan Slomp and J.J. Gilchrist in their studies on the text — to conclude it was composed centuries after the apostolic era, likely by an author or authors working in a late medieval or early modern context with access to Islamic tradition, rather than being a genuine first-century document connected to the Barnabas of Acts and Paul's missionary journeys.

## Context & Background

The canonical Gospels (Matthew, Mark, Luke, John) are dated by the large majority of critical and evangelical scholarship to the first century, within living memory of eyewitnesses, and are attested in manuscripts (e.g., papyri like P52, P66, P75) from within a century or two of composition — F.F. Bruce, *The New Testament Documents: Are They Reliable?* (1943, various editions), discusses this manuscript proximity in detail.

The "Gospel of Barnabas," by contrast, has no manuscript attestation earlier than the medieval period, is written in Renaissance-era Italian/Spanish rather than first-century Greek or Aramaic, and contains anachronisms — such as references to coinage, geography, and customs inconsistent with first-century Judea — that place its composition many centuries after the apostles. This has been documented by scholars such as Jan Slomp ("The Gospel of Barnabas in Recent Research," *Islamochristiana*, 1997) and Oddbjørn Leirvik in his studies on Muslim-Christian textual history.

The real Barnabas — Paul's companion in Acts 4, 9, 11, 13–15 — has no verified connection to this later text; the title appears to have been attached to lend it false apostolic authority, a pattern seen in other pseudepigraphal works from various eras.

## Interpretive Options

This is a general view, not a specific citation: scholars studying the "Gospel of Barnabas" — including David Sox in his book-length study *The Gospel of Barnabas* (1984) — generally place its composition somewhere between the 14th and 16th centuries, possibly by a convert moving between Christian and Islamic contexts, given its blending of Gospel narrative material with content that aligns closely with Quranic claims about Jesus (e.g., denial of the crucifixion, prediction of Muhammad). It is not treated as a serious candidate for first-century authorship by mainstream biblical or historical scholarship, and it is not accepted as canonical or authoritative by any major Christian tradition — Catholic, Orthodox, or Protestant.
