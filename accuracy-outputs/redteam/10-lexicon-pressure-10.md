## Direct Answer

Ταρταρώσας (tartarōsas) in 2 Peter 2:4 is indeed a New Testament hapax legomenon — the only occurrence of this verb in the New Testament. It's a participle from the verb ταρταρόω (tartaroō), meaning "to hold captive in Tartarus" or "to consign to Tartarus," referring to the netherworld/place of punishment for fallen divine or angelic beings in Greek thought, here applied to the imprisonment of sinning angels.

## Context & Background

Peter's use of ταρταρόω is a striking example of borrowing pagan Greek vocabulary to make a theological point. Tartarus does not appear elsewhere in the New Testament, and its background lies squarely in Greek mythology and literature — for instance, in Hesiod's *Theogony*, Tartarus is portrayed as a dark abyss beneath Hades where the Titans were imprisoned by Zeus after their rebellion (this is a widely cited literary background; see discussion in Richard Bauckham, *Jude, 2 Peter*, Word Biblical Commentary, 1983).

Bauckham and other commentators note that Jewish literature of the Second Temple period — particularly 1 Enoch 6–16 (the "Book of the Watchers") — develops a parallel and likely more immediate background: the tradition of angels ("Watchers") who sinned by taking human wives (drawing on Genesis 6:1–4) and were subsequently bound and imprisoned by God pending final judgment. Bauckham argues that Peter (and Jude, in the parallel passage Jude 6) draws on this Enochic tradition, while using the Greek term "Tartarus" as a recognizable label for the place of confinement, since Tartarus had already been used in Hellenistic Jewish literature (e.g., some Greek translations and writings) to render or gloss with concepts of a lower/dark abyss.

Gene Green (*Jude and 2 Peter*, Baker Exegetical Commentary on the New Testament, 2008) similarly notes that use of Tartarus-language reflects the wider Hellenistic Jewish practice of adapting Greek cosmological vocabulary to convey Jewish apocalyptic ideas about the fate of disobedient angelic beings, without necessarily endorsing the full Greek mythological cosmology behind the term.

## Interpretive Options

1. **Reference to fallen angels of Genesis 6** — Many commentators (Bauckham; Green) connect the "angels who sinned" in 2 Peter 2:4 to the Genesis 6:1–4 narrative of the "sons of God" and its expansion in 1 Enoch, understanding ταρταρώσας as God's act of imprisoning these specific rebellious angelic beings.

2. **General reference to Satan's rebellion/fallen angels broadly** — Some more traditional/systematic theological readings (reflected in various evangelical study Bible notes) apply this text more broadly to the fall of Satan and his angels, without tying it specifically to the Genesis 6/Watchers tradition — this is a general view, not a specific citation.

3. **Rhetorical/literary borrowing without cosmological commitment** — Green and others note Peter's use of Tartarus may be primarily rhetorical, employing a term his Hellenistic audience would recognize as "the place of confinement for cosmic rebels," without Peter thereby affirming Greek mythology's full cosmology.
