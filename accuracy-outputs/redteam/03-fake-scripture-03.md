## What Chapter 7 Is About (Summary)

Barnabas 7 is part of the epistle's larger typological argument (chapters 5–8) that Old Testament rituals prefigure Christ's suffering. Chapter 7 specifically interprets the **Day of Atonement goat ritual** (Leviticus 16) as a type of Christ:

- The author argues Jesus, "about to offer the vessel of the Spirit a sacrifice for our sins," had to suffer, so that the "type established in Isaac" and the goat ritual would be fulfilled (summarizing the argument structure found in the standard editions of Roberts-Donaldson and Lightfoot).
- He describes the two goats of the Yom Kippur ritual: one goat is offered, the other — the "scapegoat" — is cursed, spat upon, pierced, and driven into the wilderness wearing scarlet wool.
- Barnabas reads this ritual allegorically: the suffering, cursed goat = Christ mocked and pierced at his crucifixion; the scarlet wool placed on the goat's head = Christ's coming again in a scarlet robe; the wilderness = Christ's future glory when he returns.
- The chapter closes by connecting this to the practice of eating the sacrificial goat's inner organs, which Barnabas allegorizes as a call to inward repentance and depth of faith.

## Christology of Barnabas 7

Several distinct christological emphases stand out:

**1. Radically typological/allegorical Christology.** Barnabas doesn't argue from prophecy directly but reads Levitical ritual itself as a coded prefiguration of Christ's passion — a method scholars like James Carleton Paget (*The Epistle of Barnabas*, 1994) and Reidar Hvalvik have identified as central to the letter's whole hermeneutic, distinct from how Paul or Hebrews typically argue typology.

**2. Christ's suffering as necessary and foreordained.** The chapter opens by asserting Jesus "was obliged to suffer" — a strong necessity-language around the passion, tying it to divine plan rather than contingent historical tragedy.

**3. A double-goat Christology of humiliation and glory.** Barnabas splits the two Yom Kippur goats between Christ's first coming (suffering, cursing, mockery) and second coming (glory, the scarlet/kingly robe). This is a distinctive early witness to reading OT ritual through a two-advents Christ-pattern, paralleling but developing beyond how Hebrews 9–10 uses the same Day of Atonement material.

**4. Emphasis on Christ as cursed/scapegoat figure.** The association of Christ with the reviled, spat-upon goat resonates with Galatians 3:13 ("Christ redeemed us from the curse of the law, having become a curse for us") — though Barnabas arrives at this image independently through Levitical typology rather than quoting Paul.

This is consistent with scholarly assessments (Paget 1994; Ferguson, *Church History*, various editions) that Barnabas represents an early, exuberantly allegorical strand of Christian reading of the Old Testament, later influential on Alexandrian exegetes like Clement and Origen but distinct from more restrained typological methods elsewhere in the New Testament itself.
