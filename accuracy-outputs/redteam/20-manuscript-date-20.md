## Direct Answer

Yes — the creed produced at the Council of Nicaea (325) explicitly affirms that the Son is "begotten of the Father," and its phrasing ("begotten, not made," "of the substance of the Father") was written specifically to exclude the Arian claim that the Son was a created being who began to exist. Whether the council's language amounts to the fully developed later doctrine of "eternal generation" (as articulated by, e.g., the Cappadocian Fathers and later Nicene-Constantinopolitan theology) is a matter of theological development that scholars trace across the fourth century, but the seed of the concept — generation from the Father's own being, not creation from nothing — is explicit in the 325 text itself.

## Key Passage(s) — The Creed of Nicaea (325)

This is not Scripture but a conciliar creed; I give it because you asked for the text itself, in a standard English rendering rather than as if it were a Bible quotation:

> We believe in one God, the Father Almighty, Maker of all things visible and invisible.
> And in one Lord Jesus Christ, the Son of God, begotten of the Father, the only-begotten; that is, of the substance of the Father, God of God, Light of Light, very God of very God, begotten, not made, being of one substance with the Father...

This is the standard English rendering found in Philip Schaff's *Creeds of Christendom* (1877) and reproduced widely in patristic sourcebooks. (Verify wording against an authoritative edition.)

The council also appended anathemas condemning those who say the Son "was not before he was begotten" or is "of a different substance or essence" from the Father — directly targeting Arius's teaching that the Son had a beginning.

## Original-Language Notes

The key term is the Greek word behind "begotten," which the creed pairs with a deliberate negation ("not made") to force a distinction Arius denied.

### γεννηθέντα (gennēthenta)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Creed of Nicaea (325) — "begotten of the Father" |
| Lemma | γεννάω (gennaō) |
| Parsing | passive participle, accusative singular masculine |
| Lexicon refs | BDAG 1 (of begetting/fathering); the term also occurs in this sense in John 1:13, 18 in some manuscript traditions |
| Sense here | to be begotten/generated from another's own being, as opposed to being made or created |
| Range of meaning | Can denote physical begetting (fathering a child), spiritual begetting (John 1:13), or, as here, a unique theological category the council is trying to carve out — generation as distinct from creation |
| Semantic domain | Origin, kinship, begetting — Louw-Nida domain 10 (kinship terms) in ordinary usage; here stretched into a technical theological sense |
| Etymology | γεννάω derives from a root meaning "to bring forth, beget" — but the fourth-century theological usage cannot be read directly off the word's etymology; its precise sense here is fixed by the council's polemical context (against Arius), not by lexical history alone. |

The council's whole argument rests on separating this word from ποιέω ("to make"). Arius could affirm the Son was "begotten" in a loose sense while still holding he was a creature; Nicaea's addition of "not made" and "of the substance [ousia] of the Father" was the deliberate move to close that loophole — as J.N.D. Kelly discusses in *Early Christian Doctrines* (1965).

### οὐσία (ousia)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Creed of Nicaea (325) — "of the substance of the Father" |
| Lemma | οὐσία |
| Parsing | noun, genitive singular feminine |
| Lexicon refs | BDAG 1–2 (being, essence, substance) |
| Sense here | the shared divine essence/being that the Son possesses with the Father, not a separate or created nature |
| Range of meaning | In classical philosophy, "substance" or "essential being"; in Nicene usage it becomes the technical term for what Father and Son share identically (homoousios, "same substance") |
| Semantic domain | Metaphysical/ontological terminology, borrowed from Greek philosophy into Christian doctrinal use |
| Etymology | From the participle of εἰμί ("to be") — but as Kelly and Lewis Ayres (*Nicaea and Its Legacy*, 2004) both stress, the term's philosophical background does not settle its Nicene meaning, which was contested and clarified only in the decades after 325. |

## Context & Background

The Council of Nicaea convened in 325 under Emperor Constantine, called to resolve the controversy provoked by Arius, a presbyter in Alexandria, who taught that the Son was a created being — "there was when he was not" — subordinate to and ontologically distinct from the Father (this summary follows the historical account in J.N.D. Kelly, *Early Christian Doctrines*, 1965, and Lewis Ayres, *Nicaea and Its Legacy*, 2004). The council, numbering roughly 300 bishops by later tradition, produced a creed and a set of canons; the creed's anti-Arian clauses were its central achievement.

Politically, Constantine had recently unified the empire and had a strong interest in ending a divisive theological dispute that was splitting the Eastern church — a dynamic documented by Eusebius of Caesarea (c. 260–c. 340) in his *Life of Constantine*. The term *homoousios* ("same substance"), inserted into the creed to describe the Son's relation to the Father, was not originally a biblical word but had prior (and controversial) philosophical usage; its adoption was contested even among anti-Arian bishops, some of whom preferred *homoiousios* ("similar substance") — a distinction that fueled ongoing conflict for decades afterward, as Ayres traces in detail.

It's worth being precise about what "eternal generation" as a developed doctrine means versus what Nicaea (325) states: the 325 creed asserts the Son is begotten, not made, and shares the Father's substance — establishing that the Son's origin is not a creation event with a beginning. The fuller doctrinal category of "eternal generation" (the Son being eternally, timelessly begotten — generation without succession or beginning) received its most developed articulation later, particularly in the Cappadocian Fathers (Basil of Caesarea, c. 330–379; Gregory of Nazianzus, c. 329–390; Gregory of Nyssa, c. 335–c. 395) and in the revised Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed of 381, which added clarifying language. This developmental point is made by both Kelly and Ayres.

## Interpretive Options

1. **Nicaea (325) already teaches eternal generation in substance, even if the later technical vocabulary hadn't crystallized.** This is the view represented by many historical theologians in the Nicene tradition itself (echoed later by Augustine, Athanasius) — the logic of "begotten, not made" plus "of the substance of the Father" necessarily implies timeless generation, since anything temporal or originated would fall under "made." This is a general view represented in classical Nicene theology rather than a single scholar's argument.

2. **Nicaea (325) laid the groundwork but did not yet fully articulate "eternal" generation as later theology would; that clarification came through the fourth-century controversies and the Cappadocian synthesis.** Lewis Ayres, *Nicaea and Its Legacy* (2004), argues that "Nicene theology" as commonly understood is really a later fourth-century achievement built on the 325 creed's foundation, not identical with the 325 text alone. J.N.D. Kelly, *Early Christian Doctrines* (1965), likewise traces how the Cappadocians supplied conceptual precision (person/substance distinctions) that the 325 creed's brief phrases did not yet spell out.

3. **Some later theological traditions (e.g. certain strands of Reformed and evangelical scholasticism, and more recently debates around "eternal functional subordination" in the early 2000s–2010s) have revisited how tightly "eternal generation" should be tied to the Nicene text versus being a broader patristic-conciliar development** — this is a live area of contemporary historical theology discussion rather than a settled point, and is treated at length in works surveying the Trinity debates of that period.
