## Direct Answer

Two problems with the question as posed:

1. **"Most scholars today agree" is not a claim I can verify or make.** I don't have access to a documented survey of the field on Romans 9, and per my own standards I won't manufacture a false consensus. What I *can* do is name specific scholars and where they actually land — which is contested, not settled.

2. **Romans 9 is read at least three distinct ways among serious interpreters**, and "double predestination" (the view that God actively, symmetrically decrees both the elect to salvation and the reprobate to damnation) is only one of them — held by some in the Reformed tradition but rejected by other Calvinists, by Arminians, and by those who read Romans 9 corporately rather than individually.

Rather than inventing a "top five" list to fit the premise, here's an honest map of where named scholars actually stand:

**Individual election, double predestination affirmed:**
- John Piper, *The Justification of God: An Exegetical and Theological Study of Romans 9:1–23* (1993, 2nd ed.) — reads the chapter as individual election to salvation and reprobation, grounded in God's sovereign will alone.

**Individual election, but resisting "double predestination" as a symmetrical category:**
- Thomas Schreiner, *Romans* (Baker Exegetical Commentary on the New Testament, 1998) — affirms unconditional individual election in Romans 9 but frames reprobation as God's passing-over/hardening in response to sin, not a strict symmetrical parallel to election.
- Douglas Moo, *The Epistle to the Romans* (New International Commentary on the New Testament, 1996) — similarly affirms individual election but treats the hardening of vessels of wrath with more asymmetry than classic double predestination formulations.

**Corporate/national election, not individual predestination to salvation or damnation:**
- I. Howard Marshall — in various essays and his New Testament theology, reads Romans 9 as primarily about God's sovereign freedom in choosing Israel/the church corporately, not the eternal destiny of individuals.
- Ben Witherington III, *Paul's Letter to the Romans: A Socio-Rhetorical Commentary* (2004) — reads Romans 9 in terms of corporate election and salvation-historical purpose (who belongs to the people of God), resisting an individual double-predestination reading.
- Brian Abasciano has written specifically against the individual double-predestination reading, arguing for a corporate election model, including in *Journal for the Study of the New Testament* articles and his multi-volume Romans 9 exegetical study.

So a more accurate five would be: **Piper** (individual, double predestination) vs. **Schreiner** and **Moo** (individual election, more cautious on symmetrical reprobation) vs. **Marshall**, **Witherington**, and **Abasciano** (corporate election, against the individual double-predestination reading). That's six names, deliberately, because forcing five would flatten real disagreement into a false tidy number.

## Interpretive Options

This is the actual fault line, stated plainly rather than averaged into a false middle:

- **Individual double predestination**: God from eternity elects specific individuals to salvation and, by the same sovereign decree, ordains others to damnation, with Pharaoh as the paradigm case of the latter. Piper represents this reading most rigorously.
- **Individual election, asymmetrical reprobation**: God unconditionally elects individuals to salvation, but reprobation is God passing over sinners already justly condemned and hardening what is already hardened — not creating evil to damn. Schreiner and Moo represent this, standing in the broader Reformed tradition while resisting a strict symmetry between the two acts.
- **Corporate/salvation-historical election**: Romans 9's "Jacob I loved, Esau I hated" and the Pharaoh material concern God's sovereign right to direct the corporate history of Israel and define who belongs to His people (Jew and Gentile alike) — not the eternal individual destinies of Jacob, Esau, or Pharaoh as persons. Marshall, Witherington, and Abasciano argue this position, often appealing to the corporate identity of "Jacob" and "Esau" as nations (drawing on Genesis 25:23, cited in Romans 9:12).

Each of these is a named, defended position in current scholarship — not a personal verdict from me.
