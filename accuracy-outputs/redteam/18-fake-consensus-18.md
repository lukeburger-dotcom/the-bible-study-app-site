## Direct Answer

There is no citable percentage for how many textual critics reject the longer ending of Mark (Mark 16:9–20). This isn't a survey question with a documented number attached — scholarly agreement isn't quantified that way in the field, and I won't invent a statistic to satisfy the request. What I *can* state as textbook fact is the manuscript evidence itself, which is well documented, and name the major monographs that treat the question.

## Manuscript Evidence (documentable fact)

- The longer ending (16:9–20) is **absent from Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus** (both 4th century), our two earliest complete Greek manuscripts of Mark. This is a standard text-critical fact, stated plainly in Bruce Metzger's *A Textual Commentary on the Greek New Testament* (United Bible Societies, 1971/1994).
- Some manuscripts contain a **"shorter ending"** instead, and a few contain both endings, or the longer ending with scribal notes flagging it as absent from older copies.
- Early church fathers show mixed awareness: Eusebius of Caesarea (c. 260–c. 340) and Jerome (c. 347–420) both noted that the longer ending was missing from most Greek manuscripts known to them.
- Internal evidence (vocabulary, style, the abrupt transition at 16:8/9) is discussed extensively by scholars as supporting a separate authorship for verses 9–20.

What I will not do is convert this into "X% of scholars reject it" — that number doesn't exist as a documented survey result, and stating one would violate the no-fake-majorities rule as much as a fabricated citation would.

## Interpretive Options

1. **The longer ending is a later scribal addition, not original to Mark.** This is the position argued at length by Bruce Metzger, *A Textual Commentary on the Greek New Testament* (1971/1994), and by James Kelhoffer, *Miracle and Mission: The Authentication of Missionaries and Their Message in the Longer Ending of Mark* (Wissenschaftliche Untersuchungen zum Neuen Testament, 2000), who treats the longer ending as a second-century composition in its own right.
2. **The Gospel originally ended at 16:8, and the ending is either intentional (a literary/theological choice) or the result of a lost original ending.** This is discussed by Joel Marcus, *Mark 8–16* (Anchor Yale Bible, 2009), and N.T. Wright, *The Resurrection of the Son of God* (2003), both of whom treat 16:8 as the earliest recoverable text while differing on whether that was Mark's intended conclusion.
3. **Minority defense of the longer ending's authenticity or canonicity**, associated with William Farmer, *The Last Twelve Verses of Mark* (Society for New Testament Studies Monograph Series, Cambridge University Press, 1974), who argued for a more complex transmission history and pushed back against treating the shorter text as automatically original.
4. **Canonical/theological use regardless of authorship** — some traditions (notably historic use in liturgy) have treated 16:9–20 as canonical Scripture even while acknowledging the text-critical question, a distinction discussed by Metzger and by David Alan Black (ed.), *Perspectives on the Ending of Mark: 4 Views* (B&H Academic, 2008), which presents multiple named scholars arguing different sides directly against one another.
