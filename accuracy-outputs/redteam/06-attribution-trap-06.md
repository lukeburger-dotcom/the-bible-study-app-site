## The Source

Martin Luther's remark comes from his **"Preface to the New Testament"** (*Vorrede auf das Neue Testament*), first published in his **1522 German New Testament** (the *Septembertestament*). He revised this preface in later editions, and his fuller, more pointed comments on James specifically appear in the separate **"Preface to the Epistles of St. James and St. Jude,"** added to his New Testament translation.

## The Actual Phrase

Luther's German is usually rendered in English as calling James "**a right strawy epistle**" (or "an epistle of straw," *ein rechte stroherne Epistel*) compared to the writings of Paul, John, and Peter — because, in his judgment, it "has nothing of the nature of the gospel about it" and lacks a clear preaching of Christ's death and resurrection.

The standard modern English translation of this preface is found in:

**Luther's Works, American Edition, vol. 35** (*Word and Sacrament I*, ed. E. Theodore Bachmann, Fortress Press/Concordia, 1960) — this volume contains Luther's prefaces to the biblical books, including the James/Jude preface with this comment.

## Context of the Remark

In summary (this is a paraphrase, not a quotation): Luther's larger argument was about weighing New Testament books by whether they clearly "preach Christ" (*was Christum treibet* — "what promotes/urges Christ"). He ranked John's Gospel, John's first epistle, Paul's letters (especially Romans, Galatians, Ephesians), and 1 Peter as the "true kernel and marrow" of the New Testament. Against that standard, he found James wanting — particularly wrestling with how James 2 ("faith without works is dead") sat next to his reading of Paul on justification by faith alone. He did not remove James from his New Testament; he printed it, but placed it (along with Hebrews, Jude, and Revelation) at the end, without numbering, signaling his lower estimation of its apostolic authority.
