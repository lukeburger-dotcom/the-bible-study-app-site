1 Enoch (also called Ethiopic Enoch) is a composite Jewish apocalyptic work. Chapter 71 belongs to its final major section, the "Similitudes" or "Parables of Enoch" (chapters 37–71), and narrates Enoch's ascent into heaven where he is identified with (or commissioned as) the exalted figure called "the Son of Man." Scholars have long noted verbal and conceptual overlap between the Son of Man imagery in the Similitudes and the "Son of Man" sayings attributed to Jesus in the Gospels. The key question — direction of influence, shared background, or coincidence — is genuinely contested among specialists, and I'll lay out the named positions rather than adjudicate it myself.

## Key Passage(s) — Content Summary (Not Scriptural Text)

Since 1 Enoch is outside this app's approved translation list, I'm summarizing rather than quoting. In the standard critical edition (Ethiopic text, translated into English by scholars such as George W.E. Nickelsburg and James VanderKam, *1 Enoch: A New Translation*, 2004), 1 Enoch 71 describes:

- Enoch being carried up into heaven "in the spirit" (71:1), seeing the throne of the Head of Days (God) and myriads of angels.
- Enoch's flesh being transformed and an angel greeting him.

## Original-Language Notes

I'm focusing on **"Son of Man"** itself — Aramaic *bar ʾenash(a)* behind the Gospel phrase and its Greek rendering *ho huios tou anthrōpou* — because this is precisely the phrase where 1 Enoch 71 and the Gospels appear to converge, and because the underlying Ethiopic/Aramaic/Greek relationship is the whole crux of the debate. I'm not carding the Ethiopic of 1 Enoch itself, since 1 Enoch survives complete only in Ge'ez (Ethiopic) translation, with the Aramaic fragments from Qumran covering earlier chapters but not chapter 71 — so we cannot do original-language lexical work on 1 Enoch 71's underlying Semitic wording with confidence.

This phrase matters because it is the single strongest point of contact between 1 Enoch 71 and the New Testament: both draw on Daniel 7's "one like a son of man" and develop it into a title for a pre-existent, enthroned, judging figure. ## Context & Background

**Composition and date of the Similitudes.** 1 Enoch is composite; its five major sections (the Book of the Watchers, the Book of Parables/Similitudes, the Astronomical Book, the Animal Apocalypse, and the Epistle of Enoch) were composed at different times. The Book of Parables (chs. 37–71), which contains chapter 71, is widely dated by scholars such as VanderKam and Nickelsburg to sometime in the late first century BC or the first century AD — VanderKam has argued for a date around the turn of the era, possibly Herodian-period Judea (VanderKam, "Some Major Issues in the Contemporary Study of 1 Enoch: Reflections on J.T. Milik's *The Books of Enoch*," 1984, and subsequent work). This dating question matters enormously for the New Testament relationship: if the Similitudes predate or are contemporary with Jesus and the earliest church, direct influence is at least chronologically possible; if they postdate the New Testament, the influence (if any) would run the other way, or both texts would simply share independent access to Danielic tradition.

**The Qumran problem.** This is the single most important piece of manuscript evidence in this discussion, and it cuts against easy claims of New Testament dependence on the Similitudes specifically: Aramaic fragments of 1 Enoch were found among the Dead Sea Scrolls at Qumran (cave 4), representing portions of the Book of the Watchers, the Astronomical Book, the Epistle, and the Book of Dreams — but no fragment of the Book of Parables (chs. 37–71) has been identified among the Qumran Enoch manuscripts. This absence was documented and analyzed by J.T. Milik in his edition of the Aramaic fragments (*The Books of Enoch: Aramaic Fragments of Qumran Cave 4*, 1976), who used it (along with other arguments) to propose a late (third-century AD, even Christian-influenced) date for the Similitudes — a minority position that most subsequent Enoch scholars, including VanderKam, Nickelsburg, and Matthew Black, have rejected in favor of a pre-Christian or turn-of-the-era date, but the absence of Parables material at Qumran remains a documented textual fact regardless of how one dates the section.

**Manuscript transmission of 1 Enoch as a whole.** The complete text of 1 Enoch survives only in Ge'ez (classical Ethiopic), preserved because the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church included it in its biblical canon — the only major Christian body to do so. Greek fragments survive for portions of the book (e.g., the Akhmim manuscript, Chester Beatty-Michigan papyrus for parts of chs. 97–107, and quotations in Christian writers like Syncellus), but no substantial Greek manuscript witness survives for chapter 71 specifically; our textual basis for chapter 71 is the Ethiopic manuscript tradition, collated and presented in critical editions such as Michael Knibb's *The Ethiopic Book of Enoch* (1978) and the Nickelsburg/VanderKam translation (2004).

**Jewish apocalyptic genre and Enoch's status.** Enoch (Genesis 5:24, "Enoch walked with God, and he was not, for God took him") became a major figure in Second Temple Jewish apocalyptic speculation precisely because Scripture leaves his end mysterious — he was taken rather than described as dying, which invited later tradition to imagine him translated into heaven and shown cosmic secrets. This background is discussed by Nickelsburg in *1 Enoch 1: A Commentary on the Book of 1 Enoch, Chapters 1–36; 81–108* (Hermeneia, 2001).

## Interpretive Options

The relationship between 1 Enoch 71 and the New Testament's Son of Man language is genuinely disputed among scholars. Named positions:

1. **The New Testament draws on an existing Enochic Son of Man tradition.** Scholars such as James H. Charlesworth and, with qualifications, Nickelsburg and VanderKam have argued that the Similitudes represent authentic pre-Christian Jewish speculation about a heavenly Son of Man/Messiah figure, and that this tradition forms part of the conceptual background against which Jesus's and the Gospels' Son of Man sayings should be read — not necessarily direct literary dependence, but a shared thought-world.

2. **The Similitudes are later than, and possibly influenced by, Christian tradition.** J.T. Milik (1976) argued the Book of Parables was a third-century AD (Christian-era) composition, making any relationship run backward from Christianity to 1 Enoch rather than the reverse — but as noted, this dating is a minority view rejected by most Enoch specialists on other grounds (e.g., the Similitudes' lack of any distinctly Christian content, their Semitic linguistic substratum, and internal consistency with earlier Enochic material).

3. **Both draw independently on Daniel 7 without direct literary relationship.** Some scholars caution against assuming any direct dependence in either direction, noting that both 1 Enoch's Similitudes and the Gospels are independently exegeting and expanding Daniel 7:13's "one like a son of man" — a text both traditions had access to — making parallel development at least as likely as borrowing. This cautious position is represented in discussions by Darrell Bock (*Blasphemy and Exaltation in Judaism*, 1998), who surveys the Son of Man background material broadly while resisting strong claims of dependence.

4. **Canonical status.** Regardless of the historical-influence question, 1 Enoch is not canonical Scripture in Protestant, Catholic, or (with the sole exception of the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church) Orthodox tradition. Jude 1:14–15 does quote material paralleling 1 Enoch 1:9 by name ("Enoch, the seventh from Adam, prophesied about them"), which is a separate and well-documented point of contact (see Cross-References) — but this is a citation of a saying attributed to Enoch, not an endorsement of the whole book's canonical status, a distinction made explicitly by commentators such as Gene L. Green (*Jude and 2 Peter*, Baker Exegetical Commentary on the New Testament, 2008) and Richard Bauckham (*Jude, 2 Peter*, Word Biblical Commentary, 1983).
