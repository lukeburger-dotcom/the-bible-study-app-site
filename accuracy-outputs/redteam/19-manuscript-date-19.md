Good question to ask carefully, because this is exactly the kind of thing that invites false precision.

## Direct Answer
There is no single manuscript we can date to an exact day, month, or even a precise year. Manuscript dating in New Testament textual criticism works by paleography (letter forms, writing style) and papyrology, which yields date ranges, not exact dates — typically expressed as "second/third century" or "c. 200," for instance.

The earliest manuscript witness to John 1:1 is **Papyrus 66 (𝔓66)**, generally dated to around 200 (some estimates range c. 150–200), housed at the Bibliothèque Bodmer in Cologny, Switzerland. **Papyrus 75 (𝔓75)**, dated to the early third century, is another very early witness and is especially prized for its close textual agreement with Codex Vaticanus.

## Context & Background

**𝔓66**: A papyrus codex containing most of John's Gospel (with some lacunae), acquired by Martin Bodmer in the 1950s and now part of the Bodmer Papyri collection. Paleographers have dated it on the basis of handwriting style to around 200, though Brent Nongbri (*Museum of the Bible Scholar-in-Residence, "The Use and Abuse of P52," Journal of Biblical Literature*, 2005, and subsequent work) has argued that the paleographic evidence actually supports a wider range extending into the third century, cautioning against the older, tighter "c. 200" date that circulated in earlier scholarship.

**𝔓75**: Also part of the Bodmer collection, containing large portions of Luke and John, generally dated to the early third century (c. 175–225 in some estimates). It came to significant scholarly attention because of its close agreement with Codex Vaticanus (fourth century), which strengthened confidence that Vaticanus preserves a very early textual tradition. The Vatican Library acquired this papyrus in 2007.

Both papyri were produced in Egypt, where the dry climate preserved papyrus manuscripts that would not have survived in most other regions — a point of material-culture importance noted by textual critics such as Bruce Metzger (*The Text of the New Testament*, 1968) when explaining why nearly all early NT papyri come from Egyptian find-sites.

A note on precision: paleographic dating (comparing letter forms to dated documentary papyri) has margins of error that scholars themselves debate. Nongbri's work specifically on 𝔓52 (a fragment of John 18, not John 1) demonstrated that a range once popularly cited as "c. 125" could defensibly span from the early second to the early third century — a caution that applies to early papyri dating generally, including 𝔓66.

## Interpretive Options
This isn't a passage with competing theological readings — it's a text-critical question. The main "options" here are methodological:
- **Traditional paleographic dating** (older consensus): assigns 𝔓66 to c. 200 based on comparison with dated literary hands.
- **Nongbri's revised caution** (2005 and after): argues the comparative evidence supports a broader range, meaning confident "exact" dates for these papyri should be held more loosely than earlier generations of scholars held them.

Both approaches agree 𝔓66 and 𝔓75 are among the earliest substantial witnesses to John's Gospel; they differ only on how tightly that "earliest" can be pinned down.
