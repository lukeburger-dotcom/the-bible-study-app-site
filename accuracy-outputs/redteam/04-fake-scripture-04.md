# Quoting the Book of Enoch on the Son of Man, Compared to Daniel 7

A note on sourcing before we begin: 1 Enoch (specifically the "Similitudes" or "Parables of Enoch," chapters 37–71) is not part of the Protestant, Catholic, or Jewish canon. It is preserved fully only in Ge'ez (Ethiopic), and is considered canonical Scripture only within the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church. I am not confident enough in the precise wording of any English translation of the Ethiopic text to quote it verbatim without risk of error, so rather than risk misquoting a non-canonical work, I will summarize its Son of Man content accurately (attributed to the standard critical translation tradition) and quote Daniel 7 directly from our available translations, since that text **is** canon and available to quote precisely.

## Direct Answer

Daniel 7:13–14 (BSB/KJV, canonical to Jews, Catholics, Orthodox, and Protestants) describes "one like a Son of Man" coming with the clouds of heaven to the Ancient of Days, receiving dominion, glory, and a kingdom. The Similitudes of Enoch (1 Enoch 37–71), a Second Temple-period Jewish text preserved in Ethiopic and not canonical outside Ethiopian Orthodoxy, expands this figure considerably: it names a pre-existent "Son of Man" who is hidden with God before creation, will sit on a throne of glory, judge the wicked and fallen angels, and serve as light to the nations and hope for the righteous — drawing explicitly on Daniel 7's imagery but developing it into a more fully personal, pre-existent, judicial figure. Scholars debate how much of this development is presupposed by the Gospels versus how much both draw independently on Daniel 7 and other traditions.

## Original-Language Notes

The term worth isolating is the underlying Aramaic phrase in Daniel 7:13, since it is the seed of all later development — both in Enoch and in the Gospels' "Son of Man" sayings.

### כְּבַר אֱנָשׁ (Aramaic: *kevar 'enash*, "like a son of man")

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Found in | Daniel 7:13 — "One like the Son of Man" |
| Lemma | בַּר אֱנָשׁ (bar 'enash) with comparative prefix כְּ (ke-) |
| Parsing | Aramaic noun phrase (construct), masculine singular, with prefixed preposition "like/as" |
| Lexicon refs | BDB (Aramaic section) בר, אנש; HALOT אֱנָשׁ |
| Sense here | "One resembling a human being" — not a title in Daniel's own Aramaic, but a description of the figure's appearance in the vision, in deliberate contrast to the beast-like empires of Daniel 7:1–8 |
| Range of meaning | In Aramaic, "son of man" (bar 'enash) ordinarily means simply "a human being" or "someone," parallel to Hebrew ben adam; it carries no inherent messianic freight on its own |
| Semantic domain | Human identity / generic humanity vs. beast imagery |
| Etymology | "Son of" + "man/humanity" is a common Semitic idiom for membership in a category (a "son of man" = a human), not a compound conveying hidden meaning through its parts — the etymological fallacy would be to read later titular use back into the phrase's original sense. |

This phrase is the single most important interpretive hinge in the passage. In Daniel's own Aramaic it is a simile ("one *like* a son of man," i.e., "one with human appearance") contrasting with the four beasts representing empires (Dan 7:3–8). It is Second Temple Jewish interpreters — including whoever composed the Similitudes of Enoch, and later the Gospel writers — who took this figure and developed it into a titular, personal, semi-divine figure: "the Son of Man" as a name. John J. Collins (*Daniel*, Hermeneia series, 1993) traces this trajectory carefully, noting that Daniel's text itself leaves the "one like a son of man" ambiguous between a symbol for "the saints of the Most High" collectively (cf. Dan 7:18, 27) and an individual heavenly figure — a debate the Similitudes and the Gospels each resolve in the individualizing direction.

## Context & Background

**Daniel 7** — Historical and literary setting: Daniel 7 is usually dated by critical scholars to the Maccabean crisis (mid-2nd century BCE), read as addressing the persecution under Antiochus IV Epiphanes, while a traditionally conservative dating places the visions in the 6th century BCE, during the Babylonian exile, under the historical Daniel (this dating question is discussed at length in John J. Collins, *Daniel*, Hermeneia series, 1993, and in conservative commentaries such as Joyce Baldwin, *Daniel*, Tyndale Old Testament Commentaries, 1978). The chapter belongs to the apocalyptic genre, using symbolic beast-imagery drawn from ancient Near Eastern iconography (the lion, bear, leopard, and terrifying fourth beast) to represent successive empires, in contrast to the human-like figure who receives an everlasting kingdom from God.

**1 Enoch and the Similitudes** — The Book of Enoch is a composite work; the Similitudes (chapters 37–71) are generally dated by scholars such as Nickelsburg and VanderKam to the late first century BCE or the first century CE, making them roughly contemporary with or slightly prior to the New Testament writings — though this dating has been debated since no fragments of the Similitudes were found among the Dead Sea Scrolls at Qumran (unlike other sections of 1 Enoch), a point noted by J.T. Milik and subsequent scholarship (discussed in Nickelsburg, *1 Enoch 1*, Hermeneia, 2001). The work is preserved in full only in Ge'ez (classical Ethiopic), transmitted through the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church, which alone regards it as canonical Scripture. Aramaic fragments of other sections of 1 Enoch were found at Qumran, but not of the Similitudes specifically.

**Comparative ANE context**: The throne-vision imagery in Daniel 7 (thrones, fire, books opened, a divine council) has been compared by scholars to Canaanite and broader ANE divine-council scenes, and specifically to combat-myth patterns where a high god subdues chaotic sea/beast powers and enthrones a subordinate figure — a comparison developed by John Day (*God's Conflict with the Dragon and the Sea*, 1985) and discussed in relation to Daniel 7 by Collins (Hermeneia, 1993).

## Interpretive Options

**On Daniel 7:13's "one like a son of man":**
1. **Corporate/symbolic view** — the figure represents "the saints of the Most High," i.e., faithful Israel collectively, since Daniel 7:18 and 7:27 apply the kingdom to "the saints." This reading is argued by John J. Collins (Hermeneia, 1993) as at least the primary sense within Daniel's own composition.
2. **Individual heavenly/angelic figure** — the "son of man" is a distinct divine or angelic being (sometimes identified with Michael) who represents Israel before God, an interpretation with roots in some Second Temple Jewish exegesis and defended by scholars such as Collins as a secondary strand even within Daniel.
3. **Messianic/individual person** — later Jewish and Christian interpreters (including, arguably, the author of the Similitudes and the Gospel writers) read the figure as an individual, pre-existent, messianic figure. This is the New Testament's use of "Son of Man" as Jesus's characteristic self-designation (e.g., Mark 14:62, echoing Daniel 7:13 directly).

**On the relationship between Enoch's Son of Man and the Gospels:**
- Some scholars (e.g., James H. Charlesworth) argue the Similitudes represent an important, perhaps influential, pre-Christian Jewish development of Daniel 7 that illuminates the conceptual world Jesus and the Gospel writers inherited.
- Others (e.g., Nickelsburg and VanderKam, while affirming a probable first-century date) are more cautious about claiming direct literary dependence in either direction, treating both the Similitudes and the Gospels as independent developments of Daniel 7 within a shared interpretive stream.
- Because the Similitudes' precise date remains debated and it is absent from the Qumran Aramaic fragments, some earlier scholarship (mid-20th century) even questioned whether it was pre-Christian at all — a position now largely set aside given linguistic and thematic arguments in Nickelsburg and VanderKam's 2004 translation and commentary, though the fine details of dating remain a live discussion among specialists rather than a settled consensus.
