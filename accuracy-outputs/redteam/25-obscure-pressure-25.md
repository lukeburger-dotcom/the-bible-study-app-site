## Original-Language Notes

This section is less about a single Greek word and more about textual data, so rather than lexical cards, here are the manuscript-critical facts that matter, stated plainly:

**The endings that exist, as textbook-documented facts:**

1. **The abrupt ending at 16:8** — the Gospel ends with ἐφοβοῦντο γάρ ("for they were afraid"). This is the ending found in Codex **Sinaiticus** (א, 4th century) and Codex **Vaticanus** (B, 4th century).

2. **The Longer Ending (16:9–20)** — found in the vast majority of later Greek manuscripts, including Codex **Alexandrinus** (A, 5th century), Codex **Bezae** (D, 5th/6th century), and the bulk of the Byzantine manuscript tradition, as well as most ancient versions (Old Latin, Vulgate, Syriac Peshitta).

3. **The Shorter Ending** — a brief additional paragraph attached after 16:8 in a small number of witnesses (e.g., Codex **Bobiensis**, an Old Latin manuscript, itk, and a few others), sometimes standing alone, sometimes followed by the Longer Ending as well. Textbook descriptions (e.g., in the United Bible Societies' *Greek New Testament* and Nestle-Aland apparatus) note it reads roughly: the women reported briefly to Peter's companions, and afterward Jesus himself sent out through them the proclamation of eternal salvation.

4. **Some manuscripts contain both** the Shorter and Longer Endings together (e.g., Codex **Regius**, L, 8th century, and the Harklean Syriac), which is itself evidence that ancient scribes recognized multiple endings circulating and tried to preserve all of them rather than choose.

5. **Patristic evidence**: Eusebius of Caesarea (c. 260–c. 340) and Jerome (c. 345–420) both note that the Longer Ending was absent from the majority of Greek manuscripts known to them — this is a standard textbook-cited patristic testimony (cited in Metzger's *Textual Commentary*, discussed below).

I'm not presenting these as word-study cards because the interpretive weight here rests on external manuscript attestation, not on lexical semantics within the disputed verses themselves.

## Context & Background

**Manuscript tradition and dating.** Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus are both dated to the 4th century and are widely regarded among textual critics as the two earliest complete (or near-complete) Greek manuscripts of the New Testament; both lack 16:9–20 entirely, ending at verse 8. This is a standard, textbook-level fact reported in critical editions of the Greek New Testament (Nestle-Aland, 28th edition; UBS5).

**Style and vocabulary.** Bruce Metzger and others (see Sources) have noted stylistic features of 16:9–20 that differ from the rest of Mark — for example, the transition at verse 9 awkwardly re-introduces Mary Magdalene as if for the first time, even though she was just mentioned in verse 1, and the Greek vocabulary and connective style shift noticeably. This is a widely cited observation in the commentary tradition (see, e.g., discussion in Metzger's *Textual Commentary*).

**Why an ending might have been felt "missing."** Some form-critics and commentators have suggested that an abrupt ending at "for they were afraid" (ἐφοβοῦντο γάρ) struck early copyists and readers as an unsatisfying, even grammatically unusual way to conclude a Gospel, prompting later scribes to supply a conclusion. This is a **possible interpretation**, not a settled fact — R.T. France and others have argued the abrupt ending may be intentional Markan style, so the "felt need for a resolution" explanation, while common, is itself a scholarly reconstruction rather than a manuscript-attested fact.

**Geography of manuscript witnesses.** The absence of 16:9-20 is attested not only in Greek manuscripts from Egypt (where Sinaiticus and Vaticanus likely originate or were used) but also in Armenian and Georgian versions and in some Sinaitic Syriac manuscripts, showing the shorter form circulated across multiple linguistic/geographic streams — a point made in standard text-critical surveys.

## Interpretive Options

1. **The Longer Ending is a later scribal addition, not original to Mark.** This is the position of the two earliest complete Greek manuscripts (Sinaiticus, Vaticanus), reflected in the bracketing/footnoting practice of modern critical translations (BSB, and most modern committee translations). Bruce Metzger and Bart Ehrman (in Metzger's revision of *The Text of the New Testament*), and Daniel Wallace, hold this view.

2. **The Gospel originally ended at 16:8, intentionally, as a literary/theological choice** (fear and silence as the note Mark wanted to end on, inviting the reader into the resurrection proclamation). This is argued by scholars including R.T. France (*The Gospel of Mark*, New International Greek Testament Commentary, 2002) and is a common view among literary-critical readers of Mark, though it remains a **possible interpretation** of *why* the earliest text ends there, distinct from the text-critical question of *whether* it does.

3. **The original ending was lost** (e.g., the last leaf of an early copy was damaged or detached), and 16:9–20 or something like it may represent an attempt — inspired or not — to supply the lost conclusion. This is a minority proposal discussed in some older scholarship (e.g., discussed and largely set aside in Metzger's *Textual Commentary*) since there's no manuscript evidence of a damaged exemplar, only of divergent endings.

4. **Canonical/theological affirmation despite text-critical doubt** — some traditions (historically, portions of Eastern Orthodoxy and the KJV-only movement) treat 16:9–20 as canonical Scripture regardless of the manuscript question, on grounds of its long liturgical and canonical use in the Byzantine textual tradition that underlies the *Textus Receptus*. This is a documentable historical position tied to the Majority Text and Byzantine-priority approaches (associated with scholars like Maurice Robinson), not a text-critical claim about originality per se.
