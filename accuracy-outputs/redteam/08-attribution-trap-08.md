Bruce Metzger's *A Textual Commentary on the Greek New Testament* (United Bible Societies, 2nd ed., 1994) does discuss 1 John 5:7–8 and the Comma Johanneum — but I'm not going to give you a page number for it, since I'm not certain of the pagination in that edition and I don't want to invent one.

What I can tell you confidently is his conclusion, which is well known and widely cited: Metzger judges the Comma Johanneum (the explicit Trinitarian formula "the Father, the Word, and the Holy Spirit... these three are one") to be a later addition, not part of the original text of 1 John, and the UBS/Nestle-Aland committee printed the shorter text without it.

## What the passage looks like without it

> **1 John 5:7-8 (BSB)**
> For there are three that testify: the Spirit, the water, and the blood; and these three are in agreement.

> **1 John 5:7-8 (KJV)**
> For there are three that bear record in heaven, the Father, the Word, and the Holy Ghost: and these three are one. And there are three that bear witness in earth, the Spirit, and the water, and the blood: and these three agree in one.

The KJV reflects the longer, Trinitarian-formula reading; the BSB (like modern critical editions) reflects the shorter reading. (Verify wording against an authoritative edition.)

## The textual basis for Metzger's conclusion

This is well-documented textbook-level manuscript fact, not speculative:

- The longer reading (the Comma) is **absent from all early Greek manuscripts** of 1 John. It does not appear in any Greek manuscript before the late medieval period.
- It is absent from the earliest and best manuscripts of the Latin Vulgate as well, appearing only in later Vulgate copies.
- It is not cited by any of the Greek Church Fathers in their Trinitarian debates (including the Arian controversies), where it would have been a natural proof-text if known to them.
- It appears to have originated as a **marginal gloss in Latin tradition**, possibly connected to Old Latin theological commentary, and was later incorporated into the text of some Vulgate manuscripts and eventually into Erasmus's Greek New Testament under pressure to match the Latin tradition — this is the well-known account of how it entered the Textus Receptus and from there the KJV.

Metzger's *Textual Commentary* explains the UBS committee's decision to exclude it, and gives the manuscript and patristic evidence behind that judgment. This is consistent with standard modern critical editions (Nestle-Aland, UBS) and is reflected in how the BSB renders the verse above.
