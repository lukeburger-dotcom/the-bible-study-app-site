Origen of Alexandria (c. 184–c. 253) is widely associated with a form of universal reconciliation (*apokatastasis* — the "restoration of all things"), and this teaching is most closely tied to his treatise **De Principiis** (*On First Principles*, Greek *Peri Archon*), usually dated to around **220–230**, while he was working in Alexandria before his later move to Caesarea.

A few important qualifications, since this is a genuinely disputed area of Origen scholarship:

- **The Greek original of De Principiis is largely lost.** What survives mostly comes through a Latin translation by Rufinus of Aquileia (c. 345–410), made around **397–398**, and Rufinus admits in his own preface that he smoothed over passages he considered later heretical corruptions. Jerome (c. 345–420), by contrast, produced a more literal Latin translation (now lost except in fragments quoted polemically) and accused Rufinus of softening Origen's more radical statements — including on universal restoration. This is a documented ancient dispute, not a modern reconstruction.
- **Because of this transmission problem**, scholars differ on how far Origen actually taught a *guaranteed, universal* final restoration of all rational beings (including the devil) versus a more exploratory, aporetic discussion of the idea as one possibility among others he raises in the work. Henri Crouzel, *Origen* (1989), argues Origen's position is more tentative and hedged with genuine uncertainty than later critics (and later admirers) suggested. Others, treating the *apokatastasis* passages as authentically representing Origen's settled view, read him as a real universalist — this is the more common reading in older patristic scholarship going back to figures like the Emperor Justinian's condemnations.
- **Condemnation:** Propositions associated with Origen's teaching on universal restoration (along with pre-existence of souls and other points) were anathematized at a council under Emperor Justinian I in Constantinople in **543**, and again associated with the broader condemnations of "Origenism" at the **Second Council of Constantinople (553)** — though the exact relationship between that council's anathemas and Origen's actual writings is itself debated by historians (Richard Norris and others note the condemned propositions may reflect later "Origenist" monks more than Origen himself).
