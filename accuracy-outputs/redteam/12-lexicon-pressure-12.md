**BDB's stated sense**: BDB glosses עַלְמָה as "young woman (marriageable age)" — the feminine form paired with עֶלֶם (elem), "young man," both understood as denoting the vigor/ripeness of youth rather than a technical statement about sexual status. BDB derives both from a root meaning "to be full of vigor, have reached puberty," related to the idea of sexual maturity rather than virginity per se.

**HALOT** similarly treats עַלְמָה as "girl of marriageable age, young woman," noting its semantic range covers young women generally, without the term itself being the standard technical word for "virgin" — that role in Hebrew is more typically carried by בְּתוּלָה (bethulah).

## Why This Matters for Isaiah 7:14

The interpretive weight of this passage often hangs on whether עַלְמָה implies virginity as a necessary component. BDB's entry itself defines the word by age/maturity, not virginal status — though in every biblical occurrence where the woman's status is discernible (e.g., Genesis 24:43, referring to Rebekah before marriage), the young woman in view is in fact unmarried and a virgin. The Septuagint (3rd–2nd century BCE) rendered עַלְמָה here with παρθένος (parthenos, "virgin"), which Matthew 1:23 follows — but that translational choice, and its theological significance, is a distinct question from what BDB documents about the Hebrew word's own semantic range.
